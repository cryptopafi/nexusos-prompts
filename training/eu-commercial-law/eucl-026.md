# EUCL-026 — Competition Law in Digital Markets

## Problema
Traditional competition law tools are too slow for fast-moving digital markets, and businesses fail to anticipate the overlapping obligations of competition law, DMA, and sector-specific regulation.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- EUCL-019 (Art. 101), EUCL-021 (Art. 102), EUCL-030 (DMA)

### Steps
1. Identify **digital market characteristics** that amplify competition concerns: network effects (direct and indirect), economies of scale/scope, data-driven barriers to entry, multi-sided platforms, zero-price markets, tipping and lock-in
2. Apply **Art. 102 TFEU to digital dominance**: CJEU and Commission have adapted analysis — Google Shopping (AT.39740): self-preferencing as abuse; Google Android (AT.40099): tying and anti-fragmentation; Google AdSense (AT.40411): exclusivity clauses in search advertising
3. Apply **Art. 101 to platform-related agreements**: MFN/parity clauses in online distribution (Booking.com NCAs decisions), platform bans in selective distribution (Coty, C-230/16: luxury brands can prohibit Amazon sales if objective criteria), algorithmic collusion (hub-and-spoke via shared algorithm)
4. Understand **DMA as ex-ante complement** (Regulation 2022/1925): operates alongside Art. 101/102. DMA obligations are per se — no need to prove market definition or dominance. But DMA non-compliance can also inform Art. 102 analysis
5. Assess **killer acquisitions**: below-threshold acquisitions of nascent competitors. Commission uses Art. 22 referral mechanism (post-2021 guidance). Member States can refer even if no national jurisdiction. Example: Illumina/GRAIL (Commission asserted jurisdiction via Art. 22 referrals)
6. Apply **interoperability** requirements: under DMA Art. 7 (messaging interoperability for gatekeepers), under Art. 102 where refusal to provide interoperability constitutes abuse (Microsoft, T-201/04)
7. Consider **data as essential facility**: access to data may constitute essential input. Refusal to share data could be abuse under Bronner/IMS Health criteria if data is truly indispensable, refusal eliminates all competition, and no objective justification
8. Monitor **algorithmic pricing** risks: parallel algorithmic pricing may constitute concerted practice if algorithms designed to facilitate tacit coordination (Art. 101) or exploitative pricing (Art. 102)

### Verification
- Can identify digital market features that raise competition concerns
- Can distinguish DMA obligations from Art. 101/102 analysis
- Can assess when data access refusal may constitute abuse
