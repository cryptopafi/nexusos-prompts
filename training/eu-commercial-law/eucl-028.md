# EUCL-028 — IP Rights and EU Competition Law

## Problema
IP rights holders and licensees fail to navigate the tension between legitimate IP exploitation and competition law limits, particularly for standard-essential patents and technology licensing.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- EUCL-019 (Art. 101), EUCL-021 (Art. 102), EUCL-020 (BERs)

### Steps
1. Understand the **interface**: IP rights grant exclusivity, but their exercise can breach Art. 101 (restrictive licensing) or Art. 102 (abusive refusal to license). EU law distinguishes existence (protected) from exercise (subject to competition rules) — Consten and Grundig (C-56 & 58/64)
2. Apply the **Technology Transfer BER (Regulation 316/2014)**: covers patent and know-how licensing agreements. Market share thresholds: competing 20%, non-competing 30%. Hardcore restrictions include: price-fixing between competitors, output limitation, market/customer allocation (with exceptions for field-of-use and exclusive territories for non-competitors)
3. Identify **Standard-Essential Patents (SEPs)**: patents essential to implement an industry standard (e.g., 3G/4G/5G, Wi-Fi, video codecs). Holders typically commit to FRAND licensing (Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory) at the standard-setting organization
4. Apply **Huawei v ZTE** (C-170/13) framework for SEP enforcement: (a) SEP holder must alert infringer and specify how patent is infringed, (b) infringer must express willingness to negotiate FRAND license, (c) SEP holder must make written FRAND offer, (d) infringer must respond diligently with counter-offer, (e) if no agreement, parties may agree on third-party determination. Injunction only if infringer is unwilling licensee
5. Apply the **EU SEP Regulation (2023 proposal / ongoing)**: FRAND determination mechanism, essentiality checks, aggregate royalty assessment, register of SEPs. Monitor legislative progress
6. Assess **refusal to license as abuse** (Art. 102): IMS Health (C-418/01) criteria — (a) IP is indispensable to compete in downstream market, (b) refusal excludes all competition in downstream market, (c) refusal prevents emergence of new product for which consumer demand exists, (d) no objective justification
7. Review **patent settlements** under Art. 101: pay-for-delay (Lundbeck, T-472/13 — reverse payment settlements restricting generic entry are by-object restrictions if payment is incentive not to compete). Legitimate settlements possible if within patent scope
8. Consider **IP in merger review**: IP portfolios as barriers to entry, technology market definition, remedies (licensing commitments, FRAND commitments, divestiture of IP)

### Verification
- Can apply the Huawei v ZTE framework to an SEP dispute
- Can identify hardcore restrictions in technology licensing under the TT BER
- Can assess when refusal to license constitutes Art. 102 abuse
