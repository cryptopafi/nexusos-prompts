# EUCL-004 — Apply the Principle of Supremacy of EU Law

## Problema
National courts and practitioners sometimes incorrectly prioritize domestic legislation over conflicting EU law, creating compliance risks and unenforceable contract clauses.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- EUCL-001 (EU Legal System), EUCL-003 (Direct Effect)

### Steps
1. Apply the **Costa v ENEL** principle (C-6/64, 1964): EU law takes precedence over all national law, including later-enacted statutes
2. Apply **Simmenthal** (C-106/77): national courts must set aside any conflicting national provision of their own motion, without waiting for legislative repeal or constitutional court ruling
3. Recognize supremacy extends over **national constitutions** (Internationale Handelsgesellschaft, C-11/70) — though note political tensions (German BVerfG Solange doctrine, Polish/Hungarian challenges)
4. Identify the **practical consequences**: (a) conflicting national law is inapplicable (not void), (b) national courts act as EU courts of general jurisdiction, (c) no need to request constitutional review first
5. Apply to **regulations**: directly applicable, any conflicting national law disapplied automatically
6. Apply to **directives after transposition deadline**: if State failed to transpose, invoke direct effect (vertical) + supremacy to disapply conflicting national law (Ratti, C-148/78)
7. Check for **derogations**: EU law itself may permit national exceptions (e.g., Art. 36 TFEU public policy exceptions to free movement of goods, Art. 52 TFEU public policy/security for establishment)
8. In practice: advise clients that contract clauses conflicting with mandatory EU law (competition, consumer protection, GDPR) are void regardless of choice-of-law clauses

### Verification
- Can explain why a national court must disapply conflicting domestic law
- Can identify when supremacy applies to regulations vs unimplemented directives
- Can spot contract clauses that conflict with mandatory EU law
