# EUCL-003 — Apply the Principle of Direct Effect

## Problema
Failure to correctly identify when EU law provisions are directly enforceable by individuals before national courts results in missed legal remedies and unasserted rights.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- EUCL-001 (EU Legal System)
- Understanding of national court procedures

### Steps
1. Recall the **Van Gend en Loos** test (C-26/62, 1963): a provision has direct effect if it is (a) clear, (b) precise, (c) unconditional, and (d) requires no further implementation
2. Apply **vertical direct effect**: individuals can invoke EU law against the State (including emanations of the State per Foster v British Gas, C-188/89)
3. Distinguish **horizontal direct effect**: Regulations have horizontal direct effect (enforceable between private parties). Directives do NOT have horizontal direct effect (Faccini Dori, C-91/92)
4. For unimplemented directives, apply the **indirect effect** doctrine (Marleasing, C-106/89): national courts must interpret domestic law in conformity with the directive
5. Where indirect effect is insufficient, pursue **Francovich state liability** (C-6/90): Member State liable for damages caused by failure to transpose a directive if (a) directive confers rights on individuals, (b) content of rights identifiable, (c) causal link between breach and damage
6. Check whether the provision is a **treaty article** (Art. 34, 45, 49, 56, 63 TFEU all have direct effect per CJEU case law) or a **regulation** (always directly applicable, Art. 288 TFEU)
7. For **decisions**, check if addressed to individuals/companies (directly enforceable) or to Member States (vertical direct effect only)

### Verification
- Can apply the Van Gend en Loos criteria to any EU provision
- Can determine whether horizontal or only vertical direct effect applies
- Can identify the correct alternative remedy (indirect effect or Francovich) when direct effect fails
