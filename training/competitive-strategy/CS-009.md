# CS-009 — Design a Cooperative Commitment Strategy

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
When firms compete on strategic complements (pricing, advertising), aggressive commitments backfire — they trigger matching aggression from rivals, destroying value for everyone. Instead, a cooperative commitment strategy signals restraint, encouraging rivals to also moderate their competitive intensity. This is the "puppy dog" strategy: appear small and non-threatening to avoid provoking retaliation. The challenge is committing credibly to restraint while competitors are watching.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Competition on strategic complements confirmed (price, advertising intensity)
- Multi-player interaction where escalation harms all players
- Legal boundaries understood (cooperation must be tacit, not explicit — see CS-029)

### Steps
1. **Confirm strategic complements dynamics** — Verify that competitive actions provoke matching responses: when you cut prices, rivals cut prices; when you increase advertising, rivals increase advertising. The key test: does your rival's best response function slope upward (your aggression increases their aggression)? If yes, aggressive commitment destroys value. Case: Airline fare wars — one carrier's price cut triggers industry-wide matching, destroying all carriers' margins.
2. **Identify the cooperative commitment opportunity** — A cooperative commitment restricts your future competitive options, signaling to rivals that you will not escalate. This works when: (a) rivals can observe the commitment, (b) the commitment credibly limits your aggressive options, (c) rivals respond by also moderating. Case: A firm that publicly commits to premium positioning (high-end brand, premium stores, premium service) credibly signals it will not start a price war.
3. **Select the cooperation-signaling vehicle** — Options: (a) Strategic positioning (premium market segment, niche focus), (b) Capacity limitation (deliberately undersizing to signal no market-share ambitions), (c) Product differentiation investment (making your product less substitutable — reducing competitive pressure), (d) Geographic focus (concentrating in certain regions, leaving others to rivals), (e) Public communication (analyst calls, trade press — "we focus on margins, not share"). Case: Luxury brands like Hermes limit production to signal premium positioning.
4. **Apply the Fudenberg-Tirole taxonomy** — Classify your strategic situation using the 2x2 matrix:
   ```
   |                    | Strategic Substitutes | Strategic Complements |
   |Tough commitment    | Top Dog (invest to be | Mad Dog (invest to   |
   |                    | aggressive — CS-008)  | fight — usually bad) |
   |Soft commitment     | Lean & Hungry (under- | Puppy Dog (appear    |
   |                    | invest to appear weak)| small, non-threaten) |
   ```
   Cooperative commitment = "Puppy Dog" quadrant. Verify you are in the bottom-right cell.
5. **Quantify the cooperation surplus** — Calculate the difference between mutual cooperation payoffs and mutual escalation payoffs (R - P from the Prisoner's Dilemma framework, CS-004). This is the total value at stake. Your cooperative commitment should cost less than your share of this surplus. If commitment cost > cooperation benefit, the strategy is not worthwhile.
6. **Design the commitment for observability** — The commitment must be visible to rivals. Options ranked by visibility: (a) Physical/structural changes (visible capacity, store locations, product line adjustments), (b) Contractual commitments (publicly filed agreements, regulatory filings), (c) Organizational changes (restructuring that de-emphasizes volume), (d) Communication (analyst calls, press releases — least credible alone). Combine 2+ vehicles for maximum signal strength.
7. **Assess rival interpretation risk** — The danger: rivals may misread your cooperative signal as weakness and attack rather than reciprocate. Mitigate by: maintaining retaliatory capability (credible punishment if rivals exploit your restraint), signaling through strength (cooperate from a position of capability, not desperation), and using graduated moves (small initial cooperation, escalate only if reciprocated).
8. **Build the reciprocity monitoring system** — After committing to cooperation, track whether rivals reciprocate. Define measurable indicators: rival pricing stability, advertising spend trends, capacity investment pace. If rivals exploit your restraint (free-ride on your moderation), you must be prepared to withdraw the cooperative commitment and revert to competitive play. Set clear withdrawal triggers.
9. **Integrate with Porter's Five Forces** — Assess how cooperative commitment affects industry structure: Does reduced competitive intensity attract new entrants (lower barriers)? Does it shift bargaining power to buyers (less competitive options to play off)? Does it strengthen substitutes (comfortable pricing invites disruption)? Address each Five Forces concern in the commitment design.

### Verification
- Strategic complements confirmed (rival reaction functions slope upward)
- Fudenberg-Tirole taxonomy correctly applied (Puppy Dog quadrant)
- Cooperation surplus quantified and exceeds commitment cost
- Commitment vehicle selected for observability and credibility
- Rival misinterpretation risk assessed with mitigation plan
- Reciprocity monitoring system with withdrawal triggers defined
- Porter's Five Forces impact checked
