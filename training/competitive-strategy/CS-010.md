# CS-010 — Identify and Evaluate Complementary Products

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
Firms typically analyze competitors and substitutes but overlook complementary products — goods or services whose value increases when consumed together with the firm's offering. Complementors can be as strategically important as competitors: they expand your total addressable market, influence your pricing power, and create ecosystem lock-in. This procedure systematically identifies complementors and assesses their strategic impact using an extended competitive framework.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Clear definition of the firm's core product/service and its value proposition
- Customer usage data or market research on purchase patterns
- Willingness to expand strategic analysis beyond the traditional Five Forces

### Steps
1. **Extend Porter's Five Forces to include complementors** — Add a sixth force: complementors (as proposed by Brandenburger & Nalebuff in the Value Net). Complementors are the mirror image of substitutes: substitutes decrease your value, complementors increase it. Map your industry's Value Net:
   ```
   Customers (top) — YOUR FIRM (center) — Suppliers (bottom)
   Competitors (left) — Complementors (right)
   ```
2. **Identify all complementary products systematically** — Use three lenses: (a) Demand-side complements (products customers buy/use together with yours — e.g., apps for smartphones, tires for cars), (b) Supply-side complements (products that share production inputs, distribution channels, or technology — e.g., printers and ink), (c) Technology-platform complements (products built on your platform or that your product plugs into — e.g., plugins for Shopify). Case: Intel identified software developers as complementors and invested in making their CPUs easy to code for.
3. **Quantify the complementary value** — Estimate how much the complement increases willingness-to-pay for your product. Cross-elasticity of demand should be negative (complement's price increase reduces your demand). Methods: conjoint analysis (how much more will customers pay for your product when the complement exists?), historical correlation (when complement sales grew, did yours?), A/B testing (with/without complement availability).
4. **Assess the complementor's strategic position** — Use a modified Five Forces on the complement market: How concentrated is the complementor market? Can they extract value from your customers? Do they have bargaining power over you? Case: App developers are complements to Apple's iPhone, but Apple controls the App Store — extracting 30% commission from the complementor. The strategic question is who captures the complementary value.
5. **Evaluate cooperation vs. competition with complementors** — The relationship is co-opetition (Brandenburger & Nalebuff): you cooperate to grow the total market (bigger pie) but compete over value division (slice size). Map where interests align (market expansion, standard-setting, customer education) and where they conflict (pricing, data control, customer relationship ownership). Use a mini game matrix to analyze the cooperation-competition trade-off.
6. **Design the complementor strategy** — Options by integration level: (a) Market-based: let independent complementors operate freely (low control, low cost), (b) Alliance: formal partnerships with key complementors (moderate control/cost), (c) Investment: take equity stakes in complementors (high control, high cost), (d) Integration: build the complement yourself (maximum control, highest cost and risk). Choose based on strategic importance of the complement and risk of complementor hold-up.
7. **Assess complement-driven switching costs** — Complementary ecosystems create powerful lock-in: customers who invest in complements (apps, peripherals, training, data) face high switching costs. Evaluate whether your complementor strategy deliberately builds this ecosystem stickiness. Case: Microsoft Office's dominance was sustained partly by the ecosystem of templates, macros, and trained users — all complements. Link to CS-024/CS-025 (Switching Costs, Lock-In).
8. **Monitor complementor ecosystem health** — Track: number of active complementors, quality of complements, complementor investment levels, and complementor satisfaction. A dying complement ecosystem signals trouble for your core product. Case: BlackBerry's app ecosystem decline preceded and accelerated the device's market collapse. Create a complementor health dashboard with leading indicators.
9. **Plan for complementor disruption** — Assess risks: What if your key complementor becomes a competitor (vertical integration)? What if a substitute product has better complements? What if complementor innovation stalls? For each risk, develop a contingency: alternative complementors, self-build capability, or preemptive partnership locks.

### Verification
- Value Net mapped with all six forces including complementors
- Complementary products identified across demand, supply, and technology dimensions
- Complementary value quantified (willingness-to-pay increase)
- Cooperation vs. competition dynamics analyzed
- Integration level strategy selected with rationale
- Ecosystem health metrics defined and tracked
- Disruption contingencies documented
