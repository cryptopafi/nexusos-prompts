# CS-004 — Apply the Prisoner's Dilemma Framework to Business Rivalry

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
Many competitive situations — price wars, advertising escalation, capacity races — share the Prisoner's Dilemma structure where individual rationality leads to collectively worse outcomes. Without recognizing this structure, firms destroy industry profitability through mutual defection. This procedure identifies PD dynamics in business and maps pathways to cooperation or strategic defection.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Two or more firms in direct competition with interdependent payoffs
- Ability to estimate payoffs for cooperate/defect combinations
- Understanding of whether interaction is one-shot or ongoing (critical for solution approach)

### Steps
1. **Identify the Prisoner's Dilemma structure** — Verify the payoff ordering: Temptation (T) > Reward (R) > Punishment (P) > Sucker (S), AND 2R > T + S. In business terms: the biggest short-term gain comes from undercutting while your rival cooperates (T), mutual cooperation yields good profits (R), mutual defection destroys margins (P), and cooperating while being undercut is worst (S). Case: Airline price wars — if both maintain fares (R), both profit; if one cuts while the other holds (T,S), the cutter gains share; if both cut (P), industry margins collapse.
2. **Quantify the four payoff cells** — Estimate actual values for: mutual cooperation (R), mutual defection (P), temptation to defect (T), and sucker's payoff (S). Use Porter's Five Forces to validate: high buyer power increases T (easier to steal share), high barriers increase R (cooperation is more durable). Template:
   ```
   |            | Rival: Cooperate | Rival: Defect |
   |You: Coop   | R=good, R=good   | S=worst, T=best |
   |You: Defect  | T=best, S=worst  | P=bad, P=bad    |
   ```
3. **Assess the cooperation deficit** — Calculate R - P (value of cooperation over mutual defection) and T - R (temptation to cheat). If T - R >> R - P, cooperation is fragile. If R - P >> T - R, cooperation is naturally stable. This ratio determines how much "enforcement" is needed to sustain cooperation.
4. **Evaluate one-shot vs. repeated dynamics** — In a one-shot PD, defection is the dominant strategy (NE = mutual defection). But most business interactions are repeated games. Apply the Folk Theorem: if the game repeats indefinitely and players are sufficiently patient (discount factor > (T-R)/(T-P)), cooperation can be sustained as an NE through trigger strategies. Case: OPEC members face a PD on production quotas — cooperation is sustained because the game repeats and punishment (price collapse) is severe.
5. **Design a trigger strategy** — If repeated, choose between: Grim Trigger (cooperate until rival defects, then defect forever), Tit-for-Tat (mirror rival's last move), or Generous Tit-for-Tat (occasionally forgive defection). Tit-for-Tat won Axelrod's tournament because it is nice (starts cooperative), retaliatory (punishes defection), forgiving (returns to cooperation), and clear (easy for rivals to understand).
6. **Assess market transparency** — Cooperation requires detecting defection. Evaluate: Can you observe rival's actions? How quickly? With what accuracy? Porter's framework helps: in transparent industries (public pricing, regulatory filings), defection is quickly detected. In opaque industries (private contracts, complex bundling), defection is hard to spot, making cooperation harder to sustain.
7. **Identify commitment devices** — Beyond repeated play, firms can escape the PD through: capacity limitation (physically cannot overproduce), most-favored-customer clauses (contractually match any lower price), public announcements (reputation at stake), or vertical integration (changes payoff structure). Each device changes T, R, P, or S values. Link to CS-008/CS-009 for commitment strategy design.
8. **Run the SWOT/PESTLE overlay** — Check if external factors change the PD structure: new entrants (adds players, complicates cooperation), regulation (antitrust prohibits explicit cooperation but allows tacit coordination), technology disruption (changes cost structures, shifts payoffs), or demand shocks (recession increases temptation to defect). Update payoff estimates accordingly.
9. **Formulate the strategic recommendation** — Based on analysis: (a) if one-shot or cooperation is unsustainable, optimize for competitive defection while minimizing damage; (b) if repeated with transparent payoffs, signal cooperative intent while maintaining credible retaliation capability; (c) if PD structure can be changed, invest in commitment devices that transform the game.

### Verification
- PD structure confirmed (T > R > P > S and 2R > T + S)
- Payoff values estimated with documented assumptions
- One-shot vs. repeated determination made with discount factor calculation
- Trigger strategy selected with rationale
- Commitment devices evaluated for feasibility
- Antitrust constraints checked (no explicit collusion)
