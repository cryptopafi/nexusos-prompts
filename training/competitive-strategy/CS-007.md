# CS-007 — Analyze Cooperation Viability Using Repeated Game Models

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
One-shot games predict competitive destruction (mutual defection), yet many industries sustain tacit cooperation for decades. The difference is repeated interaction: when firms compete repeatedly, future punishment for defection can sustain present cooperation. This procedure determines whether cooperation is viable in a given competitive context using repeated game theory, and designs the mechanisms to sustain it within legal boundaries.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Competitive interaction confirmed as repeated (ongoing, no known end date)
- Prisoner's Dilemma structure identified (CS-004) with T > R > P > S
- Understanding of antitrust constraints in relevant jurisdictions

### Steps
1. **Confirm the repeated game structure** — Verify the interaction is genuinely repeated: same players, same strategic choices, frequent interactions, indefinite horizon. A finitely repeated game with a known end date unravels to mutual defection (backward induction from the last round). Cooperation requires either infinite repetition or uncertainty about when the game ends. Case: OPEC production decisions repeat quarterly with no known end — cooperation is theoretically sustainable.
2. **Calculate the critical discount factor** — Cooperation via trigger strategies requires the discount factor (delta) to exceed (T-R)/(T-P), where T = temptation payoff, R = cooperation reward, P = punishment. The discount factor reflects both time preference and the probability the game continues. If delta exceeds the threshold, cooperation is an equilibrium; if not, no trigger strategy can sustain it.
3. **Select the optimal trigger strategy** — Options ranked by forgiveness: Grim Trigger (harshest — permanent reversion to NE after one defection), Tit-for-Tat (mirror last action — nice, retaliatory, forgiving, clear), Generous Tit-for-Tat (forgive defection with probability p — handles noise/misperception), Win-Stay-Lose-Shift (repeat last action if payoff was good, switch if bad). For noisy environments (where actions are imperfectly observed), forgiving strategies dominate.
4. **Assess the number of players** — Cooperation difficulty scales with player count. With N players: monitoring is harder (who defected?), punishment is diluted (everyone suffers), and free-riding increases. Use PESTLE framework: regulation can substitute for monitoring (price reporting requirements). In industries with 2-4 major players (tight oligopoly), tacit cooperation is most viable. Above 6-8 players, coordination typically breaks down.
5. **Evaluate market transparency** — Cooperation requires defection detection. Assess: Are prices publicly observable? How quickly? Are volumes/capacities reported? Are customer contracts confidential? High-transparency markets (posted prices, regulatory reporting) facilitate cooperation. Low-transparency markets (private bidding, customized contracts) make defection detection slow, undermining cooperation. Apply Porter's Five Forces: concentrated buyer power increases transparency (buyers share price information).
6. **Analyze asymmetries between players** — Cooperation is hardest when players have different costs, capacities, market shares, or time preferences. The low-cost firm has less incentive to cooperate (higher temptation payoff from defection). Asymmetric firms may need asymmetric "cooperative" outcomes — the low-cost firm gets a larger share of the cooperative surplus. Case: Saudi Arabia within OPEC bears disproportionate production cuts because their cooperation reservation price is different from smaller members.
7. **Design cooperation facilitating mechanisms (legal)** — Within antitrust bounds, firms can facilitate tacit cooperation through: price leadership (one firm sets the market price, others follow), focal points (round-number pricing, standard contract terms), capacity signaling (public investment announcements), and industry associations (share aggregate data without firm-level collusion). CRITICAL: no explicit agreements on price, output, or market allocation — that is per se illegal.
8. **Stress-test cooperation stability** — Model scenarios that could break cooperation: demand shocks (recession increases temptation to cut prices), new entrant (changes game to N+1 players), technology disruption (shifts cost curves), or regulatory change (new antitrust enforcement). Use SWOT/PESTLE overlay. Identify early-warning indicators for each scenario.
9. **Build the cooperation monitoring system** — Define: What observable signals indicate the cooperative equilibrium is holding? What signals indicate defection? What is the response protocol? Create a trigger-response matrix:
   ```
   | Signal | Interpretation | Response |
   |--------|---------------|----------|
   | Rival price cut < 5% | Noise/tactical | Hold, observe next period |
   | Rival price cut > 10% | Likely defection | Match cut for 2 periods, then test |
   | New capacity announced | Potential threat | Reassess game structure |
   ```

### Verification
- Discount factor calculated and compared to cooperation threshold
- Trigger strategy selected with rationale for environment fit
- Player count and asymmetry impact assessed
- Market transparency evaluated for defection detection capability
- Legal boundary compliance confirmed (no explicit collusion)
- Monitoring system with trigger-response matrix documented
