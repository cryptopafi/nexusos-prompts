# CS-008 — Design an Aggressive Commitment Strategy

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
In sequential games, a firm that can credibly commit to aggressive behavior BEFORE rivals act can shift the equilibrium in its favor — deterring entry, capturing market share, or forcing rivals to accommodate. But commitments must be genuine (costly to reverse) and visible to be effective. Empty commitments (bluffs) are quickly exposed. This procedure designs commitment strategies that alter competitive dynamics through strategic irreversibility.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Sequential competitive interaction identified (CS-005)
- First-mover advantage opportunity assessed
- Understanding of the distinction between strategic complements and substitutes

### Steps
1. **Classify the competitive variable** — Determine whether firms compete on strategic substitutes (actions that provoke opposite reactions — e.g., quantity/capacity: if you increase, rival decreases) or strategic complements (actions that provoke matching reactions — e.g., pricing: if you cut, rival cuts). This classification determines whether aggressive commitment helps or hurts. Case: In Cournot competition (quantity/capacity), aggressive commitment to high output forces rivals to reduce theirs — beneficial. In Bertrand competition (price), aggressive commitment to low prices triggers rival price cuts — destructive.
2. **Identify the commitment opportunity** — An aggressive commitment is valuable ONLY when competing on strategic substitutes. The commitment must: (a) be visible to rivals BEFORE they act, (b) be irreversible or very costly to reverse, (c) shift your payoffs so that aggressive play becomes your best response regardless of rival action. Case: Amazon's massive warehouse network commits to fulfillment speed — rivals cannot match without similar investment, and Amazon cannot easily reverse it.
3. **Select the commitment vehicle** — Options by irreversibility: (a) Sunk capacity investment (factories, infrastructure — highest irreversibility), (b) Long-term contracts (supply agreements, customer lock-in — medium irreversibility), (c) Public announcements and reputation (CEO promises, brand positioning — low-medium irreversibility), (d) Organizational structure (sales compensation tied to volume — medium irreversibility), (e) Technology choices (platform lock-in, proprietary standards — high irreversibility).
4. **Quantify the commitment cost and strategic benefit** — The commitment has direct cost (the irreversible investment) and strategic benefit (improved competitive position from rival accommodation). Net value = strategic benefit - commitment cost. Use BCG Matrix logic: high-share players benefit most from scale commitments. Apply Value Chain analysis to find where commitment creates the largest cost advantage.
5. **Verify credibility through game-tree analysis** — Build the sequential game tree (CS-005) with and without the commitment. With commitment, verify that aggressive play is your best response at every relevant decision node (subgame perfection). If the commitment does not change your optimal action at the actual decision point, it is not credible and rivals will ignore it.
6. **Assess rival's accommodation response** — Model how rivals respond to your commitment. Rational rivals facing a credible aggressive commitment will accommodate (reduce their competitive intensity). But verify: does the rival have counter-commitment options? Can they escalate rather than accommodate? A commitment war (both sides escalate) is the worst outcome — like an arms race with no winner.
7. **Evaluate the Blue Ocean alternative** — Before committing to aggressive competition, consider whether Blue Ocean Strategy offers a better path: can you make the competition irrelevant by creating uncontested market space instead of fighting for existing market share? If differentiation is feasible, aggressive commitment may be unnecessary and wasteful. Cross-reference CS-021/CS-022 for differentiation analysis.
8. **Design the commitment execution plan** — Timeline: (a) Quiet preparation (internal investment decisions), (b) Visible signal (announcement, construction start, contract signing), (c) Completion and operation (commitment becomes fact). Timing matters: commit before rivals can counter-commit. Include escalation guardrails — define conditions under which you would abandon the commitment (exit criteria).
9. **Monitor and maintain** — After commitment, track rival responses. Verify accommodation is occurring. If rivals escalate instead of accommodating, reassess — you may be in a commitment war. Monitor for changes that could reduce your commitment's value: technology shifts making your capacity obsolete, demand drops making overcapacity costly, or new entrants ignoring the deterrent.

### Verification
- Strategic substitutes vs. complements classification confirmed
- Commitment vehicle selected with irreversibility assessment
- Game tree shows commitment changes optimal play at decision nodes
- Cost-benefit analysis positive (strategic benefit > commitment cost)
- Rival accommodation scenario validated
- Blue Ocean alternative explicitly considered
- Exit criteria defined for commitment failure
