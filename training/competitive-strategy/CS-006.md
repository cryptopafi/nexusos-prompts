# CS-006 — Evaluate Credible Threats in Market Competition

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
Competitors routinely make threats — price retaliation, patent litigation, capacity flooding — but most threats are non-credible (the threatener would not follow through because doing so harms them more than backing down). Firms that cannot distinguish credible from non-credible threats either capitulate to bluffs or ignore genuine warnings. This procedure provides a framework to assess threat credibility and design credible deterrence.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Identified competitive threat (explicit or implicit signaling)
- Sequential game context where threat precedes the threatened action
- Ability to estimate threatener's payoffs from carrying out vs. not carrying out the threat

### Steps
1. **Catalog the threat and its context** — Document: Who is threatening? What specific action? Under what conditions (trigger)? What is the threatened party expected to do in response? Case: "If you enter our market, we will cut prices to below cost." Assess whether the threat was communicated directly, through media, through pricing signals, or through capacity investments.
2. **Apply the credibility test** — A threat is credible if and only if the threatener is BETTER OFF carrying it out when the triggering condition occurs. Build a mini sequential game tree (CS-005): if the threatened action occurs, compare the threatener's payoff from executing the threat vs. not executing. If execution payoff < non-execution payoff, the threat is a bluff. Case: A monopolist threatening to cut prices below cost to deter entry is non-credible if their losses from a price war exceed the profit loss from accommodating a new competitor.
3. **Evaluate commitment devices** — Non-credible threats can BECOME credible through commitment. Assess whether the threatener has invested in making the threat self-enforcing: sunk capacity (excess production capacity makes price cutting cheaper), contractual obligations (most-favored-customer clauses force price matching), organizational incentives (sales team bonuses tied to market share, not profit), or reputation stakes (CEO publicly promised retaliation).
4. **Analyze the threatener's cost structure** — Use Porter's Value Chain to assess whether the threatener can sustain the threatened action economically. Key questions: What are their fixed vs. variable costs? What is their cash reserve? What is their cost advantage (experience curve, scale economies)? A threat to sustain below-cost pricing is credible only if the threatener has deeper pockets or lower costs than you.
5. **Assess reputation and history** — Has the threatener carried out similar threats before? In repeated competitive interactions, firms build reputational credibility. Apply the "chain store paradox" logic: even if fighting is costly in one market, a multi-market firm may fight to maintain deterrence reputation across all markets. Case: Walmart's history of aggressive price matching makes future price threats credible even when individually costly.
6. **Check for strategic irrationality** — Some threats are credible because the threatener has non-economic motives: ego, pride, market position signaling, or internal politics. A founder-CEO may fight an economically irrational battle to protect "their" market. Assess the decision-making structure: is the threat decision made by rational profit-maximizers or by agents with misaligned incentives?
7. **Design your response strategy** — Based on credibility assessment: (a) If non-credible: proceed with your planned action, prepare for post-bluff accommodation. (b) If credible: evaluate whether you can survive the threatened action, modify your action to avoid triggering, or invest in counter-commitments. (c) If uncertain: use SWOT to map scenarios and prepare contingency plans for both outcomes.
8. **Build your own credible deterrence** — If you need to make threats, apply the same framework in reverse. Invest in commitment devices BEFORE the threatening situation: excess capacity, contractual structures, public statements, or organizational incentives that make your retaliation automatic rather than discretionary. The best threat is one that never needs to be executed because the rival believes it.
9. **Monitor threat evolution** — Credibility is dynamic. Track changes in the threatener's cost structure, cash position, management team, and competitive pressures. A previously non-credible threat can become credible if the threatener's costs drop (new technology), their alternatives worsen (market contraction elsewhere), or their commitment devices strengthen.

### Verification
- Each threat mapped to a sequential game tree with payoff comparison
- Credibility rating assigned: Credible / Non-credible / Conditionally credible
- Commitment devices inventoried for both threatener and respondent
- Historical threat execution record checked
- Response strategy documented with contingency plans
- Own deterrence investments evaluated for credibility
