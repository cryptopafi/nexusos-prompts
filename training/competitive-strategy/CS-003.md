# CS-003 — Identify Nash Equilibrium in a Competitive Scenario

## Version
2.0 — Opus v2 rewrite

## Problema
Without identifying Nash Equilibria, firms cannot predict stable competitive outcomes — they either over-invest fighting battles they cannot win or under-invest in positions they could defend. Nash Equilibrium reveals the outcome where no player benefits from unilateral deviation, making it the most likely competitive resting point. This procedure provides a systematic method to find pure and mixed-strategy NE in any competitive interaction.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Game matrix constructed and simplified via dominated strategy elimination (CS-001, CS-002)
- Payoffs represent true strategic value (NPV, market share, or utility — not just revenue)
- Understanding of whether the game is one-shot or repeated (affects interpretation)

### Steps
1. **Verify game structure completeness** — Confirm the matrix captures all relevant players, strategies, and payoffs. Check for hidden players (regulators, complementors) using Porter's Five Forces — supplier and buyer power can alter payoffs. Validate that payoffs reflect long-run competitive impact, not just short-term P&L.
2. **Apply the best-response method** — For each player, identify the best response to each possible opponent strategy. In a 2-player game: for each column, circle Player 1's highest payoff (best row response); for each row, circle Player 2's highest payoff (best column response). Any cell with BOTH payoffs circled is a Nash Equilibrium.
3. **Verify NE by deviation test** — For each candidate NE, explicitly test: "If Player 1 deviates to any other strategy while Player 2 stays, does Player 1 improve?" and vice versa. If either can improve by deviating, it is NOT an NE. Case: In the classic Cournot duopoly, the NE has each firm producing where marginal revenue = marginal cost given the other's output — neither benefits from producing more or less.
4. **Handle multiple equilibria** — If 2+ pure-strategy NE exist, analyze which is more likely using focal point theory (Schelling points), Pareto dominance (both players prefer one NE), and risk dominance (which NE is safer against mistakes). Case: In technology standards (VHS vs. Betamax), both "all adopt VHS" and "all adopt Betamax" were NE — focal points and installed base determined the outcome.
5. **Check for mixed-strategy NE if no pure NE exists** — If no cell has both payoffs circled, calculate mixed strategies. For a 2x2 game, find the probability p where Player 2 is indifferent between their strategies given Player 1 mixes with probability p, and vice versa. The expected payoff at mixed NE is typically lower than the cooperative outcome.
6. **Interpret NE strategically** — Compare NE payoffs to: (a) the joint-maximizing outcome (cooperation value), (b) the minimax outcome (worst case), (c) the competitive outcome without strategic interaction. The gap between NE and joint maximum = value destroyed by competition = potential gains from coordination (link to CS-007, CS-009).
7. **Map NE to real competitive dynamics** — Translate the mathematical equilibrium to business language. Ask: "Is this equilibrium desirable? Can we change the game to reach a better equilibrium?" Options include: changing payoffs (via differentiation, CS-021/CS-022), adding strategies (innovation, CS-018), making commitments (CS-008/CS-009), or repeating the game (CS-007).
8. **Assess NE stability and robustness** — Test whether small perturbations destabilize the equilibrium. An NE is "trembling hand perfect" if it survives when players make small random mistakes. Fragile equilibria require active maintenance (monitoring, signaling) while robust ones are self-enforcing.
9. **Build a competitive equilibrium dashboard** — Summarize: current NE, payoffs for each player, deviation temptation (how much a player gains by cheating), stability rating, and strategic options to shift the equilibrium. Update quarterly or upon competitive trigger events.

### Verification
- All pure-strategy NE identified and verified by deviation test
- Mixed-strategy analysis performed if no pure NE exists
- NE compared to cooperative and minimax benchmarks
- Strategic recommendations tied to specific equilibrium properties
- Stability assessment completed for each NE
