# SE-006 — Handle Sales Objections Effectively

## Problema
Reps panic, argue, or capitulate when facing objections, destroying trust and killing deals. Objections are buying signals — they mean the prospect is engaged enough to voice concerns. 80% of sales require 5+ follow-ups after an objection, but 44% of reps give up after one (Brevet Group).

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Active deal with prospect raising concerns
- Knowledge of the 5 universal objection categories
- Documented case studies and competitive intelligence
- Pricing flexibility parameters defined by management

### Steps
1. **Build your Objection Playbook** — Catalog the 5 universal objection categories and your top 3 objections within each: (a) Price: "Too expensive," "Budget isn't approved," "Competitor is cheaper." (b) Timing: "Not right now," "Call me next quarter," "We're in the middle of [project]." (c) Authority: "I need to check with my boss," "The committee decides," "Legal has to review." (d) Need: "We're fine with what we have," "It's not a priority," "I don't see the ROI." (e) Trust: "We've been burned before," "You're too small/new," "We prefer [incumbent]." Write response frameworks for each.
2. **Apply the LAER framework** — When an objection arises: **Listen** fully without interrupting (resist the urge to answer mid-sentence). **Acknowledge** the concern genuinely: "That's a fair concern, and several of our current clients felt the same way." **Explore** deeper: "Can you help me understand what's specifically driving that concern?" **Respond** with evidence, not argument. Confirmation: "Does that address it, or is there another dimension?"
3. **Isolate before responding** — Before addressing any objection, ask: "Is that the main concern, or are there others we should discuss together?" This prevents the whack-a-mole pattern where you solve one objection and three more appear. If there are multiple, list them all, then address in priority order.
4. **Handle price objections with value reframing** — Never discount first. Instead: (a) Quantify the cost of inaction: "You mentioned losing $X/month to this problem. Over 12 months, that's $Y — our solution costs less than 3 months of that loss." (b) Break down the price: "That's $Z per user per day — less than a coffee." (c) Compare to alternatives: "Your current workaround costs [time/money]. This is actually less." Only discount as a last resort, and always attach a condition: "I can offer 15% off if you sign by Friday."
5. **Handle timing objections with urgency mapping** — Ask: "What changes between now and [their proposed timeline] that would make this a better time?" Usually, nothing changes — they're avoiding a decision. Then: "I understand. Meanwhile, the [problem] we discussed will cost approximately $X per month. What if we started with a pilot to minimize risk while you evaluate?"
6. **Handle authority objections by enabling the champion** — If "I need to talk to my boss," respond: "Absolutely. What would be most helpful for that conversation — a 1-page ROI summary, or would it be useful if I joined a brief call with them?" Then ask: "What concerns do you think [boss] will have?" Prepare the champion with answers to those concerns. Offer to create an executive summary they can forward.
7. **Handle need/status-quo objections with implications** — Use Implication questions (SPIN): "What happens if this problem isn't solved by year-end?" "How does this affect [their stated goal]?" "Last time this happened at [similar company], it cost them [X]. How would your organization handle that?" The goal is to make the cost of inaction concrete and personal.
8. **Handle trust objections with proof stacking** — Layer multiple proof types: (a) case study from their industry, (b) named reference they can call, (c) third-party review or analyst report, (d) pilot or trial offer. Trust objections require evidence, not reassurance. "I could tell you we're reliable, but here's what [Company X] said after 2 years with us."
9. **Know when an objection is a real "no"** — Not every objection is solvable. Red flags: buyer disengages after your response, refuses to schedule any follow-up, gives increasingly vague answers, or delegates to someone with no authority. If you get 3+ unresolved objections, the deal may not be real. Ask directly: "Based on our conversations, is this something you genuinely see moving forward, or has something changed?"
10. **Log and analyze objections monthly** — Track in CRM: objection type, your response, outcome (resolved/unresolved/deal lost). Monthly, analyze: which objection type kills the most deals, which responses work best, which objections indicate a qualification problem (should have been caught earlier). Update your Objection Playbook quarterly.

### Verification
- Objection Playbook created with 15+ pre-written responses (3 per category)
- LAER framework applied consistently (listen first, not argue first)
- Isolation question asked before responding to any objection
- Objections logged in CRM with response and outcome
- Monthly objection analysis completed
