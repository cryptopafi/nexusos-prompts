# SE-003 — Close the Sale (Ask for the Order)

## Problema
68% of sales reps never ask for the order (HubSpot). Fear of rejection, poor timing, and lack of closing techniques leave deals in perpetual "thinking about it" limbo. Closing is a skill, not a personality trait.

## Procedura
### Prerequisites
- Deal is qualified (BANT/MEDDIC criteria met — see SE-016)
- Buyer has confirmed value alignment and addressed objections (SE-006)
- Proposal or pricing has been presented
- Decision-maker identified and engaged

### Steps
1. **Confirm readiness signals before closing** — Look for buying signals: buyer asks about implementation timeline, discusses internal rollout, introduces other stakeholders for approval, asks about contract terms, or says "when we start..." If 2+ signals present, the buyer is ready. Do NOT close if the buyer is still evaluating alternatives or hasn't confirmed budget.
2. **Summarize agreed value** — Before asking, recap: "So we've agreed that [solution] will solve [their specific pain], deliver [quantified outcome], within [timeline], for [investment]. Does that capture everything?" This is the Trial Close — if they correct something, address it. If they nod, proceed.
3. **Choose your closing technique based on buyer type** — Match the close to the buyer's decision style: **Analytical buyer**: Assumptive Close — "Shall I send the contract for Tuesday start?" **Driver buyer**: Direct Close — "Are you ready to move forward?" **Amiable buyer**: Summary Close — "Based on everything we've discussed, this seems like a strong fit. What do you think?" **Expressive buyer**: Urgency Close — "The Q2 pricing expires Friday. I'd love to lock this in for you."
4. **Handle the pause** — After asking, STOP TALKING. Count to 10 silently. The first person who speaks after a close attempt loses leverage. Silence is uncomfortable but powerful. Let the buyer process and respond.
5. **Navigate the three responses** — (a) YES: confirm next steps immediately — "Great, I'll send the contract today. Can you sign by [date]?" (b) OBJECTION: this is not a no, it's a request for more info. Use SE-006 LAER framework. (c) NO/NOT NOW: ask "What would need to be true for this to make sense?" to reveal the real blocker.
6. **Use the "Two Paths" close for stalled deals** — "I see two paths: Path A is we start the pilot next month and you see results by Q3. Path B is we revisit in 6 months, but the problem we discussed will have cost you approximately $[X] by then. Which path makes more sense for your team?"
7. **Secure commitment with micro-commitments** — If the buyer won't sign today, get the smallest possible next commitment: "Can we schedule the legal review for Thursday?" or "Can you introduce me to procurement this week?" Each micro-commitment tests real intent vs. polite stalling.
8. **Document the close in CRM immediately** — Within 1 hour: update deal stage, log close attempt and response, set next action with date, note any new stakeholders or requirements. If closed-won: trigger handoff to implementation. If not closed: schedule follow-up within 48 hours.
9. **Send a post-close confirmation email** — Within 2 hours: "Thanks for [meeting]. To confirm: [summarize agreement]. Next steps: [specific actions with owners and dates]. Please reply to confirm." Creates written record and keeps momentum.
10. **Debrief and pattern-match** — After every close attempt, spend 5 minutes answering: What technique did I use? What was the buyer's reaction? What would I do differently? Track close attempt-to-win ratio (benchmark: 40-60% for qualified deals). Review monthly for patterns.

### Verification
- Closing technique selected based on buyer type, not default
- Post-close email sent within 2 hours
- CRM updated with close attempt details and next steps
- Close attempt-to-win ratio tracked and baselined
