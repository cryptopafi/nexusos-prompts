# Cornell Service Excellence Standards — Staff Protocols & Quality Benchmarks

## Obiectiv
Establish measurable service excellence standards across all guest-facing operations using Cornell's services marketing framework, the SERVQUAL model adapted for boutique hospitality, and structured staff development protocols. Define the gap between guest expectations and service delivery, close it systematically, and build a team culture where excellence is the default — not the exception. Target: zero preventable service failures per month, 95%+ positive review sentiment, and staff retention above 80% annually.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Define the Service Standards Matrix
- Create a Service Standards Matrix covering every guest-facing function. Cornell's framework uses five SERVQUAL dimensions adapted for hospitality:
  - **Tangibles**: physical appearance of the property, equipment, staff grooming, printed materials. Standard: every guest-visible surface is clean and maintained, staff wears clean, pressed uniforms or a defined dress code, all printed materials (menus, welcome cards, maps) are current and undamaged.
  - **Reliability**: ability to deliver the promised service consistently. Standard: if the booking says "breakfast included from 08:00–10:00," breakfast is ready at 07:55 and available until 10:05. Zero exceptions without prior guest communication.
  - **Responsiveness**: willingness to help and speed of service. Standards: phone answered within 3 rings, in-person guest acknowledged within 30 seconds, room service delivered within 25 minutes, maintenance request responded to within 30 minutes (acknowledged) and 2 hours (resolved or guest updated).
  - **Assurance**: staff knowledge, courtesy, and ability to inspire trust. Standard: every staff member can answer the top 20 guest questions without hesitation (see Step 3), handle payments correctly, and explain all property offerings.
  - **Empathy**: individualized attention and genuine care. Standard: staff use guest names after the first interaction, remember dietary restrictions without being reminded, and adapt communication style to the guest's mood and energy.
- For each dimension, define 3–5 specific, observable behaviors with measurable thresholds. "Good service" is vague. "Guest greeted by name within 30 seconds of approaching the desk" is auditable.

### Pas 2: The Service Promise Document
- Write a one-page Service Promise that every team member signs. This is the internal commitment — not a marketing document. It states in plain language what the property guarantees to every guest, every time:
  - "We will greet you by name and with genuine warmth."
  - "We will never say 'that's not my department' — we will own your request until it is resolved."
  - "We will deliver what we promised, when we promised it. If we cannot, we will tell you before you notice."
  - "We will resolve any problem within 30 minutes or give you a clear update on when it will be resolved."
  - "We will remember what matters to you — your room preference, your coffee order, your child's name."
- The Service Promise is posted in the staff area (not guest-facing). It is read aloud during onboarding and referenced during performance reviews. When a service failure occurs, the question is: "Which promise did we break, and what system failed?"

### Pas 3: The Top 20 Guest Questions — Staff Knowledge Standard
- Compile the 20 questions guests ask most frequently. Every staff member must be able to answer all 20 without checking a reference. Test quarterly.
- Typical Top 20 for a rural boutique property like Albastru/Șapte Focuri:
  1. What time is breakfast/dinner?
  2. What is the Wi-Fi password?
  3. Where can I park?
  4. Is there a restaurant nearby / what do you recommend?
  5. What time is check-out?
  6. Can I get an early check-in / late check-out?
  7. Where is the nearest ATM / pharmacy / gas station?
  8. What activities are available nearby?
  9. Can you book a table at the restaurant for us?
  10. What is on the menu tonight?
  11. Do you have options for dietary restrictions (vegetarian, gluten-free)?
  12. Is it safe to drink the tap water?
  13. What is the best walking/hiking trail nearby?
  14. Can we visit the kitchen / watch the cooking?
  15. Where can we buy local products (honey, jam, wine)?
  16. How far is it to [nearest town, nearest attraction]?
  17. Can you arrange a taxi / transfer?
  18. Is there phone signal here? Which carrier works best?
  19. What is the weather expected to be this week?
  20. Can we extend our stay?
- Create a laminated reference card for new staff (first 30 days only — after that, answers must be memorized). Update the list seasonally as guest questions shift.

### Pas 4: Service Recovery Protocol — The LEARN Framework
- Service failures will happen. The difference between a 3-star and a 5-star property is not the absence of problems but the speed and quality of recovery. Cornell's service recovery research shows that a well-recovered failure can produce higher loyalty than a flawless experience (the "Service Recovery Paradox").
- Implement the LEARN recovery framework:
  - **L**isten: let the guest express their frustration completely. Do not interrupt, do not defend. Maintain eye contact and open body language. "I understand — please tell me exactly what happened."
  - **E**mpathize: acknowledge the guest's feeling, not just the fact. "I can see how frustrating that must have been — that is not the experience we want for you."
  - **A**pologize: sincere, specific apology. "I'm sorry that the hot water was not working this morning." Not: "I'm sorry you feel that way" (which is not an apology).
  - **R**esolve: fix the problem immediately if possible, or provide a clear timeline. Offer compensation proportional to the severity:
    - Minor inconvenience (slow service, missing amenity): verbal apology + immediate fix + small gesture (complimentary coffee, dessert). Cost: 0–30 RON.
    - Moderate failure (room issue requiring room change, significant delay): apology + fix + room upgrade or discount on current stay (10–20%). Cost: 50–150 RON.
    - Major failure (double booking, safety concern, ruined special occasion): apology + complete fix + significant compensation (complimentary night, full meal credit, future stay voucher) + personal follow-up from owner/manager. Cost: 200–500 RON.
  - **N**otify: log the incident, the resolution, and the cost. Share with the team so the root cause is addressed. If the same failure occurs 3 times, it is a system problem — not bad luck.
- Empower front-line staff to execute LEARN up to the "Minor" compensation level without seeking approval. For "Moderate" and above, the Duty Manager or owner decides — but the acknowledgment and empathy steps happen immediately, regardless of authorization level.

### Pas 5: Staff Hiring — The Attitude-First Protocol
- Cornell's hospitality leadership research (Kate Walsh) emphasizes: "Hire for attitude, train for skill." Technical skills (PMS operation, table setting, cooking techniques) can be taught. Genuine warmth, attention to detail, and work ethic cannot.
- Interview protocol for hospitality roles:
  - **Scenario question**: "A guest approaches you looking upset and says their room smells musty. What do you do?" — Listen for: does the candidate instinctively empathize first, or jump to solutions? Do they take ownership, or deflect?
  - **Detail test**: during the interview, leave a piece of paper on the floor near the entrance. Does the candidate pick it up? This simple test (used by many luxury hotels) reveals attention to environment.
  - **Energy assessment**: hospitality work requires sustained positive energy during long shifts. Ask: "Tell me about a time you had to be cheerful when you really were not." Look for honest self-awareness, not performative enthusiasm.
  - **Reference check**: ask previous employers specifically: "Would you trust this person alone with a difficult guest? Why or why not?"
- For Albastru/Șapte Focuri, prioritize candidates from the local community when possible — they bring authentic knowledge of the area, local language nuances, and a personal connection to the place. A local staff member who says "My grandmother used to make this jam" is worth more than a trained hospitality graduate reciting a script.

### Pas 6: Onboarding — The First 14 Days
- Day 1: Welcome and property tour (as if they were a guest). Share the property story — why it exists, what it stands for, who the typical guest is. Introduce the Service Promise. Assign a "buddy" — an experienced team member who shadows and mentors for 7 days.
- Days 1–3: Observe and learn. The new hire shadows every role: front desk check-in, housekeeping room preparation, restaurant service, kitchen operations. They do not serve guests independently yet. They observe how the team interacts, what the standards look like in practice, and ask questions.
- Days 4–7: Supervised practice. The new hire performs their primary role with the buddy present. After each guest interaction, a 2-minute debrief: "What went well? What would you do differently?"
- Days 8–14: Independent with check-ins. The new hire works independently with twice-daily check-ins from the buddy. Any guest complaint during this period is a learning opportunity, not a failure.
- Day 14: Onboarding review meeting. Assess: Top 20 Questions test (must pass 18/20), Service Promise understanding (can they explain each point?), observed guest interaction quality (buddy's assessment), and the new hire's own feedback on what confused them or felt unclear.
- Ongoing: monthly 15-minute 1:1 with manager for the first 6 months. Simple agenda: "What is going well? What is frustrating you? How can I help?"

### Pas 7: Daily Service Rituals
- **Morning Briefing (10 minutes, before first guest interaction)**:
  - Today's arrivals: names, special occasions, dietary restrictions, VIP flags.
  - Yesterday's feedback: one positive review highlight, one improvement area.
  - Today's focus: one specific service standard to emphasize (e.g., "Today, let's make sure every guest is greeted by name at every interaction").
  - Weather and local events: so staff can proactively advise guests.
  - Any operational issues: maintenance, supply shortages, staffing changes.
- **Shift Handover (5 minutes)**:
  - Active guest issues and their status.
  - Rooms with special requests or ongoing needs.
  - Anything the incoming shift must know to maintain seamless service.
- **Evening Debrief (5 minutes, after last guest interaction)**:
  - What went well today? (Specific: "Mrs. Ionescu said our dinner was the best she's had in Romania.")
  - What could have been better? (Specific, not personal.)
  - Any guest issues unresolved for tomorrow?
- These rituals take 20 minutes total per day. They prevent 80% of service failures caused by communication gaps.

### Pas 8: Quality Auditing — The Mystery Guest & Self-Audit
- Conduct a quarterly self-audit using the Service Standards Matrix from Step 1. Score each dimension 1–5. Be honest — self-audits only work if the team is willing to rate themselves below 5.
- Twice annually, arrange a "Mystery Guest" — a friend, a trusted contact, or a professional auditor who books and stays as a regular guest. They evaluate the complete experience against the standards and provide a detailed written report. The mystery guest should not be known to staff.
- Mystery Guest evaluation form should cover:
  - Booking process (ease, response time, information quality)
  - Pre-arrival communication (timeliness, personalization)
  - Arrival experience (greeting, check-in speed, welcome amenity)
  - Room quality (cleanliness, amenities, comfort, maintenance)
  - F&B experience (food quality, service speed, staff knowledge, ambiance)
  - Staff interactions (warmth, name usage, problem-solving)
  - Departure experience (checkout speed, farewell quality, review prompt)
  - Overall: "Would you recommend this property to a friend? Why or why not?"
- Review mystery guest reports in a team meeting. Celebrate strengths, create action plans for weaknesses. Track improvement between mystery guest visits.

### Pas 9: Performance Management — Recognition & Accountability
- Monthly "Service Star" recognition: identify one team member who delivered exceptional service (based on guest feedback, team nomination, or manager observation). Recognition is public (announced at morning briefing, posted in staff area) and includes a small reward (50–100 RON bonus, a meal at a local restaurant, a day off).
- Quarterly performance review focused on service behaviors, not just tasks completed:
  - Guest feedback mentioning the staff member by name (positive and negative).
  - Top 20 Questions test score.
  - Observed compliance with the Service Standards Matrix.
  - Initiative: did they identify and fix a problem before being asked?
  - Team contribution: do colleagues enjoy working with them?
- Accountability for service failures is about systems, not blame. "The guest waited 20 minutes for breakfast because [the kitchen was understaffed / the order ticket was lost / the server forgot]" leads to fixing the system. "The guest waited because Maria is slow" leads to resentment and hidden failures.
- Termination standard: repeated failure to meet service standards after documented coaching, or a single incident of dishonesty, rudeness, or safety violation. The bar for joining is high; the bar for staying is fair but firm.

### Pas 10: Continuous Improvement — The Service Excellence Flywheel
- Service excellence is not a destination but a flywheel: deliver excellent service → receive positive reviews → attract higher-quality guests → justify premium pricing → invest revenue in staff and property → deliver even better service.
- Monthly: review all guest reviews. Categorize feedback into the 5 SERVQUAL dimensions. Identify the dimension with the most negative mentions — that is the priority for the next month.
- Quarterly: update the Service Standards Matrix based on accumulated feedback. Standards that are consistently met (100% compliance for 3+ months) can be raised. Standards that are consistently missed need root cause analysis — is the standard unrealistic, or is the execution failing?
- Annually: benchmark against competitors. Book a stay at 2–3 comparable properties (similar location, similar positioning, similar price range). Experience their service as a guest. Identify what they do better and what you do better. Steal good ideas shamelessly — adapt, do not copy.
- Track the financial correlation: monitor ADR, RevPAR, and direct booking percentage alongside service scores. The data will show that service improvement drives revenue — this is the business case that justifies continued investment in staff training and property maintenance.

## Note
- **Common mistake — writing standards nobody reads**: a 30-page service manual that sits in a drawer is worthless. The Service Promise is one page. The Top 20 Questions fit on a card. The Morning Briefing is 10 minutes. Keep everything actionable and brief.
- **Common mistake — punishing errors instead of fixing systems**: a team that fears punishment hides problems. A team that is empowered to report and fix problems improves. Cornell's research on high-performing teams (Kate Walsh) is unambiguous: psychological safety predicts service quality.
- **Edge case — staff shortage on a busy day**: when you are understaffed, prioritize the Moments of Truth (arrival, Signature Moment, departure) and let administrative tasks slip. A guest will forgive slow billing but will not forgive a cold welcome.
- **Edge case — cultural or language barriers**: for international guests who do not speak Romanian or English, have a translation app ready (Google Translate camera mode works well for menus). A warm smile and genuine effort to communicate transcends language perfectly.
- **Albastru / Șapte Focuri context**: for a property with a small team (likely 3–8 staff), every person is cross-functional. The Service Standards Matrix should reflect this — the person who cleans rooms might also serve dinner. Training must cover all guest-facing roles, not just the primary one. The advantage of a small team is that communication is fast and personal — the disadvantage is that one sick day can break the system. Build redundancy: every critical function must be performable by at least 2 people.
- **Cornell pricing connection**: Cornell's services marketing module (Sheryl Kimes) proves that service quality directly enables premium pricing. A rural property cannot compete on location convenience or brand recognition — it competes on service and experience. Every RON invested in service training has a measurable return through higher ADR and direct bookings.
