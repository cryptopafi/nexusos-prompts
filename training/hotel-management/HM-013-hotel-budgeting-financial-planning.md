# Hotel Budgeting and Financial Planning

## Obiectiv
Build an annual operating budget that serves as the financial roadmap for the property — aligning revenue targets, cost controls, staffing plans, and capital expenditure into a single coherent plan. The budget must be completed by November for the following year, reviewed monthly against actuals, and reforecasted quarterly to ensure the property maintains financial discipline and achieves a target GOP margin of 30–45%.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Gather Inputs (September–October)
- **Revenue inputs**:
  - Full-year actuals for the current year (YTD + projection for remaining months based on OTB and forecast per HM-004).
  - Revenue forecast model for the upcoming 12 months (HM-004), broken down by month, by segment (HM-006), and by channel (HM-007).
  - ADR and occupancy targets by season, incorporating the seasonal calendar (HM-020) and pricing strategy (HM-005).
  - F&B revenue projection: forecasted covers x average spend per cover, by outlet and by meal period.
  - Other revenue projections: spa, activities, parking, retail — budget conservatively at last year's actuals +/- known changes.
- **Cost inputs**:
  - Current year actual costs by category (USALI format per HM-012).
  - Department head submissions: each department head submits a cost estimate for their department for the upcoming year with line-by-line justification. Deadline: October 15. Items that exceed current year actuals by more than 10% require written justification.
  - Staffing plan: every position, contract type, rate, hours, and seasonal adjustment (per HM-012 Pas 5). Include social contributions (CAS, CASS — approximately 37.25% on top of gross salary in Romania), meal vouchers, and any benefits.
  - Known price increases: supplier contract renewals, utility rate changes, insurance premium adjustments, software subscription renewals.
  - Inflation adjustment: apply the forecasted inflation rate (BNR publishes quarterly forecasts) to all cost lines unless a specific known price is available.
- **CAPEX inputs**:
  - List of all planned capital expenditures: renovations, equipment purchases, IT upgrades, vehicle acquisition, furniture replacement.
  - For each CAPEX item: estimated cost, funding source (cash reserves, bank loan, lease), timeline, and expected impact on revenue or costs.
- **External inputs**:
  - Economic outlook: GDP growth, consumer confidence, exchange rates (relevant if you have international guest segments paying in EUR).
  - Tourism market forecasts: national and regional tourism statistics from INSSE, WTTC, and local tourism boards.
  - Competitor activity: known new openings, renovations, or closures in your competitive set.
  - Regulatory changes: minimum wage increases (typically announced by December in Romania for the following year), tax rate changes, new compliance requirements.

### Pas 2: Build the Revenue Budget
- **Room Revenue Budget** = Budgeted Occupancy x Rooms Available x Budgeted ADR, calculated month by month:
  - For each month, estimate: target occupancy (based on seasonal calendar, forecast model, and STLY data), target ADR (based on pricing strategy and expected demand mix), and calculate: Target RevPAR = Occ x ADR, Room Revenue = RevPAR x Rooms Available x Days in Month.
  - Break revenue down by segment: what proportion comes from Direct, OTA, Corporate, Group, Retreat, Local? Apply the segment mix targets from HM-006.
  - Break revenue down by room type: if Deluxe rooms carry a 30% ADR premium over Standard, the room type mix matters for overall ADR.
  - Cross-reference: do the segment targets x segment ADRs sum to the overall ADR target? If not, adjust until they are consistent.
- **F&B Revenue Budget**:
  - Breakfast revenue: occupied rooms x breakfast capture rate x breakfast price per person x average persons per room (1.5 for couples-heavy properties, 2.5 for family-heavy).
  - Dinner revenue: occupied rooms x dinner capture rate x average dinner spend per person x average persons per room. For a rural property with a strong culinary concept, dinner capture rate should be 50–70%.
  - Lunch revenue (if served): estimated covers x average lunch spend. Lunch is typically lower-volume for rural properties.
  - Bar/beverage revenue: estimate as a percentage of total F&B (typically 20–30%).
  - Event/private dining revenue: based on confirmed bookings and historical average.
  - External guests (non-staying diners): estimate based on restaurant capacity and marketing plans.
- **Other Revenue Budget**: spa, activities, parking, retail. Budget at last year's actuals unless a specific growth initiative is planned (e.g., launching a new cooking class program).
- **Total Budgeted Revenue** = Room Revenue + F&B Revenue + Other Revenue.

### Pas 3: Build the Expense Budget
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**:
  - F&B food cost: apply target food cost % (28–32%) to budgeted food revenue.
  - F&B beverage cost: apply target beverage cost % (20–28%) to budgeted beverage revenue.
  - Housekeeping supplies and amenities: CPOR x budgeted occupied room nights.
- **Labor Cost** (the largest and most complex line):
  - List every position with monthly cost: gross salary + social contributions (CAS 25%, CASS 10%, CIM 2.25%) + meal vouchers + other benefits.
  - Apply seasonal staffing plan: core staff (12 months), seasonal staff (May–September or property-specific), casual staff (specific events/peaks).
  - Add overtime budget: estimate 3–5% above base payroll for unplanned overtime.
  - Add training budget: 1–2% of total payroll for training, certifications, and development.
  - **Total labor should be 25–35% of total budgeted revenue.** If it exceeds 35%, review staffing levels or revenue projections.
- **Marketing Budget**: typically 5–8% of revenue for independent properties.
  - OTA commissions: estimated OTA room revenue x average commission rate (15–18%). This is often the single largest marketing cost.
  - Paid advertising: Google Ads, Google Hotel Ads, social media ads. Allocate by channel based on ROI data from the previous year.
  - Photography/videography refresh: budget annually for professional content updates.
  - Website maintenance: hosting, booking engine subscription, SEO.
  - Email marketing tool subscription.
  - PR/influencer: budget for 2–4 hosted stays for food bloggers/influencers per year.
- **Property Operations & Maintenance**: 3–5% of revenue.
  - Routine maintenance: planned preventive maintenance schedule costs.
  - Repair reserve: 1–2% for unplanned repairs.
  - Contracted services: elevator, HVAC, pest control, fire safety.
  - Grounds maintenance: garden, pathways, exterior.
- **Utilities**: estimate based on last year's consumption adjusted for known rate changes and any energy efficiency improvements planned.
- **Administrative overhead**: accounting fees, legal fees, software subscriptions, office supplies, bank charges, insurance.
- **Contingency reserve**: 2–3% of total revenue. This is the buffer for unforeseen costs. If not used, it flows directly to GOP.

### Pas 4: Calculate the Projected P&L — The Financial Test
- Compile the monthly P&L using the USALI structure:
  - **Total Revenue**
  - Minus: COGS = **Gross Profit** (target: 65–72% of total revenue)
  - Minus: departmental expenses (rooms, F&B, other departments)
  - = **Total Departmental Operating Profit**
  - Minus: undistributed operating expenses (A&G, marketing, maintenance, utilities)
  - = **GOP (Gross Operating Profit)** (target: 30–45% of total revenue)
  - Minus: management fees (if applicable)
  - = **Income Before Fixed Charges (IBFC)**
  - Minus: fixed charges (rent, property taxes, insurance, depreciation, loan interest)
  - = **Net Operating Income (NOI)** (target: 15–30% for owned properties)
  - Minus: income tax
  - = **Net Profit**
- **Financial health tests**:
  - If GOP < 25%: the cost structure is unsustainable. Challenge every variable expense line. Identify the 3 biggest cost reduction opportunities.
  - If payroll exceeds 35% of revenue: overstaffed for the revenue level. Either increase revenue (pricing, marketing) or reduce staff (difficult but sometimes necessary).
  - If F&B food cost exceeds 35%: menu pricing is too low, portions are too large, waste is too high, or supplier costs are uncompetitive.
  - If cash flow is negative in any month: ensure you have sufficient working capital or a credit facility to cover the gap. Hospitality is seasonal — negative cash flow months are normal in low season but must be planned for.

### Pas 5: Cash Flow Forecast — The Survival Document
- **A profitable business can still fail if it runs out of cash.** This is especially true in hospitality where revenue is seasonal but many costs are year-round.
- Build a 12-month cash flow forecast showing:
  - **Monthly cash inflows**: room revenue collected (when guests pay, not when they stay — prepayments arrive before the stay, post-stay invoices arrive after), F&B cash and card receipts, other revenue.
  - **Monthly cash outflows**: payroll (paid monthly, on specific dates), supplier payments (typically net 30), utilities (monthly), rent/mortgage (monthly), insurance (annual or quarterly), tax payments (quarterly or annual), loan repayments (monthly), CAPEX payments (per project timeline).
  - **Net cash flow**: inflows minus outflows. Calculate the cumulative cash position at month-end.
- **Critical months**: identify any month where the cumulative cash position drops below a safety threshold (typically 1 month of fixed costs). For those months, plan in advance:
  - Draw on a credit line (arrange before you need it — banks lend when you do not need money, not when you do).
  - Defer non-essential CAPEX.
  - Accelerate collection of outstanding invoices (corporate accounts).
  - Pre-sell gift vouchers or packages (generates cash inflow before the stay occurs).
- **Typical cash flow pattern for a seasonal rural property in Romania**:
  - Cash-positive months: June, July, August, September, December (holidays).
  - Cash-neutral: April, May, October.
  - Cash-negative: January, February, March, November.
  - The summer surplus must fund the winter deficit. If it does not, the business model needs adjustment (increase winter demand per HM-020, reduce winter fixed costs, or secure seasonal financing).

### Pas 6: CAPEX Planning — Investing in the Future
- CAPEX (Capital Expenditure) is spending on assets that generate value over multiple years: building renovation, furniture, equipment, technology.
- For each CAPEX item, document:
  - **Description**: what is being purchased or renovated.
  - **Cost estimate**: obtain quotes from 2–3 suppliers for items above 5,000 RON.
  - **Funding source**: cash reserves, bank loan (commercial rates in Romania: 5–10% for SMEs), leasing (for equipment), or government grant/subsidy (check PNRR, HoReCa investment programs, Casa Verde for eco-investments).
  - **Timeline**: when will the work be done? Ideally during low season to minimize revenue disruption.
  - **Expected useful life**: for depreciation calculation. Furniture: 5–7 years. Equipment: 3–5 years. Building renovation: 10–20 years.
  - **Revenue impact**: will this investment increase revenue? "Renovating 3 rooms allows us to reclassify them as Deluxe, commanding 150 RON more per night. At 60% occupancy, annual incremental revenue = 32,850 RON."
  - **Cost impact**: will this investment reduce costs? "Replacing the old boiler with a high-efficiency model reduces gas cost by 2,500 RON/month = 30,000 RON/year. Payback: 18 months."
  - **Payback period**: Total CAPEX cost / Annual incremental revenue (or cost savings). Target: under 3 years for revenue-generating investments, under 5 years for cost-reduction investments.
- **CAPEX does not appear in the operating P&L as an expense** — it is depreciated over the asset's useful life (annual depreciation appears as a fixed charge). However, it affects cash flow significantly, which is why the cash flow forecast (Pas 5) is essential.
- **Priority ranking**: rank all CAPEX items by payback period and strategic importance. Fund the fastest-payback items first unless a strategic project (e.g., adding rooms) takes priority.

### Pas 7: Monthly Budget vs. Actual Review
- By the 5th of each month, the accountant/GM produces the Budget vs. Actual report for the closed month.
- For every P&L line item: Budgeted | Actual | Variance (RON) | Variance (%) | Explanation.
- **Significant variance threshold**: 10% or 5,000 RON (whichever is smaller). Variances beyond this threshold require written explanation and corrective action.
- **Quarterly reforecast**: at the end of each quarter (March, June, September), take actuals YTD + revised forecast for remaining months = full-year reforecast. Compare the reforecast to the original budget.
  - If the reforecast shows revenue shortfall > 5% vs. budget: activate a cost containment plan. Identify which variable costs can be reduced without impacting guest experience (casual labor hours, marketing spend reallocation, deferred non-essential purchases).
  - If the reforecast shows revenue surplus > 5% vs. budget: consider accelerating CAPEX investments or increasing marketing spend to capture additional demand.
- **Annual budget performance review**: present in January for the prior year. Format: (1) budget vs. actual summary for the full year, (2) top 3 positive variances and root causes, (3) top 3 negative variances and root causes, (4) key lessons that should inform next year's budget assumptions, (5) recommendations for the current year's budget adjustments.

### Pas 8: Financial Reporting to Ownership
- **Monthly owner report** (2 pages maximum):
  - Page 1: P&L summary (revenue, GOP, NOI) with budget comparison. Key operational metrics (occupancy, ADR, RevPAR). Cash position.
  - Page 2: narrative summary — what happened this month, what is the outlook for next month, any decisions needed from ownership.
- **Quarterly business review** (30-minute meeting or presentation):
  - Financial performance vs. budget and reforecast.
  - Competitive position update.
  - CAPEX progress and any budget adjustments needed.
  - Strategic initiatives update.
  - Risks and opportunities for the remaining year.
- **Annual investor/owner presentation** (60 minutes):
  - Full-year financial results.
  - Market analysis and competitive position.
  - Next year budget overview and strategic plan.
  - CAPEX proposal for owner approval.
  - 3-year financial outlook.
- **Transparency builds trust**: owners who receive regular, honest financial reporting are more supportive during difficult periods and more willing to invest in growth opportunities.

## Note
- **A budget without monthly monitoring is theater.** The value of the budget is in the monthly comparison and the corrective actions it triggers — not in the initial document. A property that builds a budget in November and does not look at it again until the following November has wasted the effort.
- **Common mistake — underbudgeting peak-season labor**: in hospitality, last-minute staffing (agency workers, emergency overtime) costs 30–50% more than planned staffing. Budget for the peak staff level you will actually need, at planned rates. The cost of over-budgeting labor by 5% is far less than the cost of scrambling with 20% premium agency labor.
- **Common mistake — optimistic revenue, conservative costs**: this combination produces a budget that looks profitable on paper but fails in execution. Be realistic on both sides. If in doubt, budget revenue at 90% of your best estimate and costs at 105%.
- **Edge case — first year of operations**: if the property is new with no history, build the budget using: industry benchmarks for your property class, competitor revenue estimates (reverse-engineer from review volume and ADR observations), and conservative occupancy assumptions (40–50% year 1). Accept that year 1 will have high variance — the budget's value is in establishing the discipline of monthly comparison and learning.
- **Albastru / Sapte Focuri context**: for a property in the startup or early growth phase, the budget is both a financial plan and a business case validation tool. Key assumptions to stress-test: (1) Can the property achieve 55% annual occupancy with the current room count and demand generation plan? (2) Does the F&B operation break even independently (restaurant revenue covers F&B costs), or is it subsidized by room revenue? Ideally, F&B is profitable standalone — for a property with a unique culinary concept, this should be achievable. (3) Is the cash flow positive on a full-year basis, even with seasonal troughs? If not, what is the working capital requirement and how is it funded? These questions should be answered clearly in the budget before committing to operations.
