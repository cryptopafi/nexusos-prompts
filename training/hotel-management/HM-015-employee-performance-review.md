# Employee Performance Review Framework

## Obiectiv
Implement a structured, fair, and actionable performance management system that motivates high performers, develops average performers into strong performers, addresses under-performance decisively, and aligns individual behavior with property service standards — targeting 90%+ of staff rated "Meets Expectations" or above, with zero surprises in formal reviews due to consistent ongoing feedback.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Define Performance Criteria — The Role Scorecard
- Every role must have a clear, documented Role Scorecard with measurable criteria. Create the Scorecard during the hiring process (HM-014) and review it during onboarding (Day 14).
- **Criteria categories**:
  - **Quantitative KPIs** (50% of the score — objective, data-driven):
    - Front Desk: average check-in time (target: <8 min), upsell conversion rate (target: 20%+), check-in accuracy (zero folio errors), shift audit variance (target: <10 RON).
    - Housekeeping: rooms cleaned per shift (target: 12–14), inspection score average (target: 8.5+/10), turnaround time for departure rooms (target: <40 min), zero guest complaints within 2 hours of check-in.
    - F&B Service: average spend per cover achieved vs. target, table turn time, allergen compliance (zero incidents), POS accuracy (zero voids due to order errors).
    - Kitchen: food cost % adherence (actual vs. recipe card), prep completion before service, HACCP compliance (temperature logs complete), waste reduction trend.
    - Maintenance: request response time (target: <4 hours), preventive maintenance schedule adherence (90%+), repeat issue rate (same problem recurring after "fix").
  - **Behavioral Standards** (30% of the score — observed by supervisor):
    - Attendance and punctuality (zero unexcused absences, <3 late arrivals per review period).
    - Grooming and uniform compliance (per property standard, daily).
    - Guest interaction quality (greeting, eye contact, name usage, body language, proactive service).
    - Team collaboration (helps colleagues, communicates effectively, contributes to team culture).
    - Adherence to SOPs (follows documented procedures consistently).
    - Initiative (identifies problems and suggests solutions without being asked).
  - **Development Goals** (20% of the score — growth-oriented):
    - Skills acquired during the review period (e.g., learned wine service, completed PMS advanced training).
    - Training completed (internal or external courses, certifications).
    - Progress toward career development objectives set in the previous review.
- Format: create a Google Sheets template with rows = criteria, columns = Target | Self-Assessment Score (1–4) | Manager Assessment Score (1–4) | Evidence/Notes. One template per role, reused for every review.

### Pas 2: Continuous Feedback — The Foundation
- **Formal reviews are only as useful as the continuous feedback that precedes them.** A surprise in a formal review indicates a failure of day-to-day management. If you cannot give specific examples from the last 4 weeks to support your review assessment, you have not been observing closely enough.
- **Real-time feedback** (daily, 15 seconds each):
  - Positive: "The way you handled that difficult guest at check-in was excellent — you stayed calm, you empathized, and you solved it immediately. That's exactly what we want." Deliver in the moment, within earshot of colleagues (public recognition reinforces the behavior for the whole team).
  - Corrective: "When a guest approaches the desk, I need you to make eye contact and acknowledge them within 10 seconds, even if you're busy with something else. I noticed you missed that twice this morning." Deliver privately, specifically, and without emotion. Focus on the behavior, not the person.
- **Weekly 1-on-1** (10 minutes, every staff member with their direct supervisor):
  - Format: 3 questions. (1) "What went well this week?" (2) "What was challenging?" (3) "What's one thing you want to focus on next week?"
  - The supervisor adds one observation: one specific thing the employee did well or one thing to improve.
  - Document briefly (3 lines in a shared note). These weekly notes become the evidence base for the formal review.
  - Schedule: same day, same time every week. Consistency matters — a cancelled 1-on-1 tells the employee "you're not important."
- **Behavior log**: supervisors keep a running log of significant observations (positive and negative) per employee. Format: date, observation, context. This log prevents recency bias in formal reviews (only remembering the last 2 weeks) and provides specific evidence for every assessment.

### Pas 3: Formal Review Structure (Bi-Annual)
- **Frequency**: bi-annual for confirmed staff (mid-year review in June/July + year-end review in December/January). Quarterly for probationary staff (30/60/90-day per HM-014).
- **Pre-review preparation** (1 week before the review meeting):
  - **Manager**: review the Role Scorecard, compile KPI data for the 6-month review period (pull from PMS, upsell tracker, inspection logs, complaint log), review the behavior log and weekly 1-on-1 notes, prepare 3 specific positive observations and 2 development observations.
  - **Employee**: complete a Self-Assessment form (same criteria as the Role Scorecard — they rate themselves 1–4 on each criterion and provide brief comments). Return to the manager 2 days before the review. The self-assessment ensures the employee arrives at the review having reflected on their own performance.
- **Review meeting** (45–60 minutes, private room, no interruptions):
  1. **Open positively** (5 minutes): acknowledge 2–3 specific achievements from the review period. Use evidence from the behavior log. "In the last 6 months, your upsell conversion rate has been 27%, which is the highest on the team. And I particularly remember how you handled the Jones family complaint in October — that was textbook recovery."
  2. **Review KPI performance** (15 minutes): go through each quantitative metric. Share the data. Discuss reasons for variances — both over-performance and under-performance. "Your average check-in time is 9.2 minutes against a target of 8. What do you think is causing the extra time?" Listen to the employee's perspective.
  3. **Behavioral assessment** (10 minutes): discuss observed behaviors with specific examples. No generalizations. "You need to be more professional" is useless. "On November 12, when a guest asked for late checkout and you said 'that's not possible' without offering an alternative — that's not the service standard we've agreed on. What could you have said instead?"
  4. **Self-assessment comparison** (10 minutes): compare the employee's self-assessment against the manager's assessment. Focus on the gaps — these are the richest discussion points. If the employee rates themselves a 4 and the manager rates 2, explore why. Is the employee unaware of the standard? Is the manager's evidence unclear?
  5. **Development goals** (10 minutes): agree on 3 specific goals for the next 6 months. Use SMART format (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). Examples: "Complete the wine service certification by April." "Achieve a minimum upsell conversion rate of 22% for each of the next 6 months." "Lead the team briefing independently at least once per week for the next quarter."
  6. **Compensation discussion** (year-end review only, 5 minutes): link the overall performance rating to the salary adjustment. Be transparent about the link: "Based on your performance rating of 3 — Meets Expectations — your salary increase for next year is 5%."
  7. **Close** (2 minutes): summarize the agreed goals. "I'm pleased with your progress and I believe you have strong potential here. Let's focus on the wine service certification and the upsell conversion, and we'll check in on these in our weekly 1-on-1s." Thank the employee for their effort.

### Pas 4: Rating Scale and Calibration
- Use a **4-level rating scale** (avoid 5-level — the middle option becomes the default that everyone receives):
  - **4 — Exceeds Expectations**: consistently above KPI targets by more than 15%. Demonstrates initiative, leadership, and problem-solving beyond the role requirements. A role model for peers. Actively contributes to improving team performance. Top 10–15% of staff should receive this rating.
  - **3 — Meets Expectations**: meets all KPI targets consistently. Adheres to SOPs. Reliable, competent, professional. Responds well to feedback and shows steady improvement. This is the standard — 60–70% of staff should be here.
  - **2 — Needs Development**: partially meets targets. Specific, identifiable gaps in performance or behavior. Requires additional support, training, or closer supervision. Not failing — but not at the standard. Supported by a Development Plan. 15–20% of staff.
  - **1 — Below Expectations**: fails to meet minimum standards despite documented support and feedback. Immediate Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) required. Continued rating of 1 in the following review period leads to termination discussion. Less than 5% of staff — if more than 5% are rated 1, the hiring or management process has failed.
- **Calibration session** (annual, all supervisors/department heads together, facilitated by GM):
  - All managers present their team ratings and the evidence supporting each rating.
  - Cross-department discussion ensures consistency: a "4" in Housekeeping must reflect the same standard as a "4" in F&B.
  - Prevent rating inflation (everyone gets a 3) and rating deflation (harsh managers give 2s where 3s are warranted).
  - Output: agreed final ratings for every employee, documented and signed by the calibrating managers.

### Pas 5: Development Planning — Growth for Every Rating Level
- **Rating 4 (Exceeds)**: identify a stretch opportunity — a skill or responsibility that develops them toward their next role. "You've mastered front desk operations. For the next 6 months, I'd like you to shadow the revenue management process — learning rate decisions and forecast review — as preparation for a supervisor role." Provide priority access to external training and conferences.
- **Rating 3 (Meets)**: identify one growth area and one deepening area. Growth: a new skill to learn. Deepening: an existing skill to elevate to excellence. "You're solid on check-in operations. Let's work on your upsell technique — I'd like to get your conversion from 20% to 28%. And I'd like you to start learning the night audit process."
- **Rating 2 (Needs Development)**: create a written Development Plan:
  - Specific gaps identified (with evidence).
  - Support provided: additional training, mentoring, observation of a high-performer, adjusted workload during development period.
  - Timeline: 90 days to demonstrate measurable improvement.
  - Success criteria: specific, measurable targets that define "improvement achieved."
  - Check-in frequency: bi-weekly during the development period.
  - Document signed by employee, supervisor, and GM. The signature is not agreement — it is acknowledgment that the plan has been communicated and understood.
- **Rating 1 (Below Expectations)**: create a **Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)**:
  - The PIP is a formal document that precedes termination consideration.
  - Content: specific behaviors/performance that must change, exactly how they must change, the support that will be provided, the timeline (30–60 days), and the consequence of non-improvement ("If these standards are not met by [date], we will proceed with employment termination").
  - Meet with the employee to present the PIP. Be direct but respectful. "This is not a punishment — it's a structured plan to help you reach the standard we need. We want you to succeed, and this plan gives you every support to do that. But I also need to be honest: if these specific areas don't improve by [date], we won't be able to continue the employment."
  - PIP must be acknowledged and signed by the employee. If the employee refuses to sign, note "presented but employee declined to sign" with a witness.
  - Legal note: in Romania, dismissal for performance reasons requires documented evidence of underperformance, a documented improvement opportunity (PIP), and compliance with the Labor Code notice period. A well-documented PIP is essential legal protection.

### Pas 6: Compensation Linked to Performance
- **Salary adjustment guidelines** (apply at the year-end review):
  - Rating 1 (Below Expectations): 0% increase. Focus is on the PIP.
  - Rating 2 (Needs Development): 0–2% increase. Minimal adjustment reflecting below-standard performance.
  - Rating 3 (Meets Expectations): 3–6% increase. This should at least match inflation to maintain real purchasing power.
  - Rating 4 (Exceeds Expectations): 6–10% increase. Significant recognition of outstanding contribution.
- **Non-monetary recognition** (equally important for motivation):
  - Employee of the Month: visible board in staff area, certificate, and tangible reward (100–200 RON bonus, hotel dinner for two, preferred parking spot for the month).
  - Annual performance awards: ceremony at the year-end team event. Categories: Best Guest Service, Most Improved, Team Player of the Year, Rising Star (best new hire).
  - Career progression: rating 4 employees get first preference for promotions, cross-training opportunities, conference attendance, and leadership development.
  - Shift preference: consistent top performers can be offered first choice of shift preferences where scheduling allows.
- **Team-based incentives** (in addition to individual):
  - If the property's Booking.com review score increases by 0.2+ points in a quarter, the entire team receives a bonus (100 RON per person or a team dinner). This aligns individual effort with collective outcome.
  - If the property achieves the monthly RevPAR budget, a small team celebration (pizza night, cake, early close by 30 minutes on a low-demand day).

### Pas 7: Post-Review Follow-Through — Where Most Systems Fail
- **Documentation**: all review forms are signed by both manager and employee. Filed in the employee's personnel folder (physical or digital, GDPR-compliant — access restricted to HR, direct supervisor, and GM).
- **Goal tracking**: the 3 agreed development goals from the review are entered into a shared tracker (Google Sheets or PMS HR module). Each goal has a specific milestone and check-in date.
- **Weekly 1-on-1 integration**: development goals are referenced in the weekly 1-on-1 conversations. "How is the wine certification study going? Do you need any support?"
- **Mid-period check** (3 months after the formal review): informal review of goal progress. Adjust if circumstances have changed (employee promoted, role changed, property priorities shifted).
- **Manager accountability**: if a manager consistently does not complete reviews on time, does not document feedback, or does not follow through on development plans, this is reflected in the manager's own performance review. Performance management is a management responsibility, not an HR exercise.

### Pas 8: Special Situations
- **Seasonal staff reviews**: for staff on 4–6 month seasonal contracts, conduct a single end-of-season review (in the last 2 weeks of their contract). The review serves two purposes: (1) feedback for the employee's development, (2) documentation for the re-hire decision ("invite back next season" vs. "do not re-hire").
- **New hire reviews**: follow the 30/60/90-day probation structure per HM-014. These are more frequent and more focused than the bi-annual reviews for confirmed staff.
- **Promotion reviews**: when considering an employee for promotion, conduct a promotion assessment separate from the regular review. Assess: current role performance (must be consistently 3 or 4), readiness for the new role's responsibilities, and development areas that would need support in the new role. A great server is not automatically a great F&B supervisor — assess management potential specifically.
- **Underperformer who does not improve**: if a PIP does not result in improvement after 60 days, proceed with termination per Labor Code procedures. Document the entire journey: initial feedback, Development Plan, PIP, check-in records, and final assessment. This documentation is essential for legal compliance and protects the property from wrongful dismissal claims.

## Instrumente
- Role Scorecard template (Google Sheets — one per role, with KPI targets and rating columns)
- Self-Assessment Form (Google Forms for digital submission, or printed for face-to-face review)
- Performance Review Record (Google Docs — structured template for the review conversation, signed and filed)
- Development Plan template (Google Docs — gaps, support, timeline, success criteria)
- PIP template (Google Docs — formal, with legal compliance language, signed by employee and manager)
- Weekly 1-on-1 Notes log (Google Sheets or simple document — date, 3 questions, one observation)
- Behavior Log (per supervisor — running observations per employee)
- Personnel folder (physical or digital, GDPR-compliant — all review documents, contracts, certifications)
- Compensation adjustment tracker (annual, linked to rating and budget)

## Note
- **The most common failure: reviews conducted with no follow-through.** If development goals are set but never mentioned again, staff learn that reviews are theater and stop engaging. Follow-through in the weekly 1-on-1 is the mechanism that makes the formal review meaningful.
- **Feedback must be specific, timely, and behavioral.** "You need to be more professional" is useless. "When a guest approached you at 14:00 and you didn't look up from your phone for 15 seconds — that's not the welcome standard we agreed on. Next time, please put the phone down immediately and greet them" is actionable.
- **The self-assessment is not a formality**: employees who rate themselves honestly often identify development areas the manager missed. Employees who consistently over-rate themselves may lack self-awareness — this is itself a development area.
- **Albastru / Sapte Focuri context**: in a small property with 5–8 staff, the owner/GM does all reviews personally. This is an advantage — personal attention from the owner signals that each person's contribution matters. Block 2 full days per year for reviews (1 day for mid-year, 1 day for year-end) and treat them as non-negotiable. Staff who feel seen, valued, and developed in a small property have significantly lower turnover than equivalent roles in large chain hotels. The personal relationship between owner and team is the most powerful retention tool available — leverage it through consistent, honest, caring performance conversations.
