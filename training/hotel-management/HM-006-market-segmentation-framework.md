# Market Segmentation Framework

## Obiectiv
Identify, quantify, and target distinct guest segments so that pricing, marketing spend, channel strategy, and service design are tailored to the highest-value segments — enabling differentiated acquisition strategies rather than a one-size-fits-all approach, with the goal of growing the top 3 segments' net revenue contribution by 10–15% year-over-year.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Identify Your Segment Universe
- Pull 12 months of reservation data from PMS and classify every booking into one of these primary segments. If the PMS does not support segment tagging, export to a spreadsheet and tag manually — this one-time effort creates the foundation for all revenue and marketing decisions.
- **Primary segments** (start with these, refine after year 1):
  - **Leisure Individual (FIT — Free Independent Traveler)**: individual, couple, or family traveling for leisure. Booked direct or via OTA. This is typically the largest segment for rural/boutique properties (40–60% of bookings). Sub-segment by channel: FIT-Direct (highest value) vs. FIT-OTA (lower net ADR due to commission).
  - **Leisure Group**: 5+ rooms booked under a single reservation for leisure purposes. Wedding parties, family reunions, friend groups, tour groups. Higher operational complexity but fills the property. Average LOS often 2–3 nights.
  - **Corporate Individual**: single business traveler. Company-negotiated rate or corporate credit card. Typically Monday–Thursday. Low F&B capture (they expense modestly). Valuable for filling midweek troughs.
  - **Corporate Group/MICE**: meetings, incentives, conferences, events. 5+ rooms plus event/meeting space. The highest total-revenue-per-booking segment when F&B and event fees are included. Booking window: 2–6 months. Requires dedicated sales outreach.
  - **Retreat/Wellness**: guests specifically seeking retreat experiences — yoga retreat, digital detox, cooking school, wellness program. Often booked through specialist platforms (BookRetreats.com, retreat directories). Longer booking windows (1–3 months). Higher willingness to pay for curated experiences.
  - **Foodie/Gastro-tourist**: guests whose primary travel motivation is the culinary experience. They discovered the property through food media, Instagram, word-of-mouth, or food blogs. High F&B spend. Often willing to travel further than the average leisure guest. The most organic segment for a property with a unique culinary concept.
  - **Local/Staycation**: guests from within 100km who book for a short escape (typically 1–2 nights on weekends). Price-sensitive but high repeat potential. Low acquisition cost if reached through local social media and partnerships.
  - **Long Stay**: bookings of 5+ nights. Digital nomads, remote workers, relocating professionals, artists-in-residence. Negotiate a lower nightly rate but guarantee revenue volume and low housekeeping cost (less frequent cleaning).
  - **OTA Promotional / Deal-seeker**: guests who booked using a discount promotion, flash sale, or loyalty deal (Booking.com Genius, Airbnb special offer). Price-sensitive with low loyalty — they will switch properties for a 10% price difference. Lowest net ADR and lowest repeat rate. Necessary for filling low-demand periods but not a segment to grow deliberately.
- Tag each booking in the spreadsheet with its segment code (FIT-D, FIT-OTA, GRP, CORP, MICE, RET, FOOD, LOCAL, LNG, PROMO).

### Pas 2: Quantify Each Segment — Build the Segment Performance Table
- For each segment, calculate these metrics using the 12-month data:
  - **Volume**: total room nights per segment and percentage of total room nights.
  - **Gross ADR**: average daily rate per segment.
  - **Net ADR**: Gross ADR minus commission and discount costs. Example: FIT-OTA at 350 RON ADR with 18% Booking.com commission = 287 RON Net ADR. FIT-Direct at 350 RON ADR with 2% card fee = 343 RON Net ADR. The difference (56 RON per room night) is why growing direct is so important.
  - **Booking Window**: average days between booking date and arrival date. This determines when your marketing and pricing actions need to happen.
  - **Length of Stay (LOS)**: average nights per booking. Longer LOS = lower per-night acquisition and housekeeping cost.
  - **Ancillary Spend**: average F&B + experience + extras spend per room per stay. For a property with a strong F&B concept, ancillary can equal or exceed room revenue.
  - **Cancellation Rate**: percentage of bookings that cancel before arrival. OTA segments typically have 20–30% cancellation rates; direct bookings 5–15%. High cancellation rates mean you need to overbook the segment to achieve the target volume.
  - **Repeat Rate**: percentage of bookings from guests who have stayed before. Repeat guests have near-zero acquisition cost and typically spend 15–25% more than first-timers.
  - **Total Net Revenue per Segment** = (Volume x Net ADR) + Total Ancillary Spend. This is the ranking metric.
  - **Guest Lifetime Value (CLV)**: for segments with measurable repeat rates, estimate CLV = Net Revenue per Stay x Expected Number of Stays over 5 years. The Local/Staycation segment often has the highest CLV despite lower per-stay spend because they visit 3–6 times per year.
- Build a **Segment Performance Table** in Google Sheets with all these columns. Sort by Total Net Revenue to identify your top 3 segments by value.

### Pas 3: Build Segment Profiles — Know Your Guest
- For each of the top 3–4 segments, create a detailed profile document:
  - **Demographics**: age range (estimate from observation and booking data), origin (domestic city, country — PMS captures nationality), travel purpose, group composition (solo, couple, family, friends).
  - **Psychographics**: what motivates them? Price? Unique experience? Nature? Food? Relaxation? Status? Understanding motivation determines your marketing message.
  - **Booking behavior**: preferred channel (OTA name, direct website, phone), device (mobile vs. desktop — Booking.com extranet shows this data), lead time, day-of-week preference, preferred room type, rate sensitivity (do they book standard or upgrade?).
  - **Decision drivers**: what factors determine their choice? Review the top 5 positive review keywords from guests in each segment. Common drivers by segment:
    - FIT-OTA: price, photos, review score, location convenience.
    - Foodie: culinary uniqueness, chef reputation, menu quality, Instagram-worthiness.
    - Corporate: location proximity to meeting venue, Wi-Fi quality, check-in speed, quiet room.
    - Retreat: program quality, facilitator credentials, property ambience, group suitability.
  - **Pain points**: what do they complain about? Review the top 5 negative review keywords per segment. Common: FIT complains about value-for-money; Corporate complains about check-in wait; Retreat complains about noise or lack of serenity.
  - **Upsell receptivity**: which upgrades and add-ons does each segment purchase? FIT responds to room upgrades. Foodie responds to tasting menus and wine pairings. Corporate responds to late checkout. Retreat responds to spa and wellness add-ons.
- Use OTA review data (Booking.com review categories), your own post-stay surveys, and front desk observations to enrich these profiles.

### Pas 4: Define Segment Strategy — Tailored Approach per Segment
- For each priority segment, define a strategy across four dimensions:
  - **Acquisition channel**: where and how to reach them.
    - FIT-OTA: Booking.com profile optimization (photos, description, response rate), Genius program enrollment. Airbnb for the "unique stay" positioning.
    - FIT-Direct: Google Hotel Ads, SEO for "[region] boutique hotel", email marketing to past guest list, Instagram (12K followers is a strong asset).
    - Foodie: food bloggers and influencers (leverage existing influencer relationships), Instagram food content, Google My Business optimization for restaurant searches, TripAdvisor restaurant listing, food media outreach.
    - Corporate/MICE: direct outreach to companies within 100km (IT companies in Pitesti, Bucharest satellite offices), LinkedIn, partnership with event planners and team retreat agencies.
    - Retreat: BookRetreats.com listing (primary OTA for this segment), retreat directories (RetreatGuru, BookYogaRetreats), partnerships with retreat facilitators who bring their own groups.
    - Local: Facebook and Instagram ads targeting within 100km radius, local partnerships (gym chains, wellness centers), Google Maps local listing optimization.
  - **Rate strategy**: dedicated rate plan for each segment.
    - FIT: BAR with seasonal dynamic pricing (HM-005).
    - Corporate: negotiated annual rate (BAR-10 to BAR-15%) with minimum volume commitment (e.g., 50 room nights per year).
    - Group: package rate including room + F&B + activity, 10–15% below sum of individual components.
    - Retreat: all-inclusive package rate (room + meals + program space + facilitator accommodation) quoted per person per night.
    - Long Stay: weekly rate (7 nights for the price of 6) or monthly rate (30% below BAR).
    - Local: "Dinner + Stay" package — competitive package price that includes dinner at Sapte Focuri + room + breakfast. This captures the dine-and-stay impulse.
  - **Service customization**: what does each segment need that others do not?
    - Corporate: fast Wi-Fi (guarantee 25+ Mbps), desk workspace in room, early checkout option, quiet room assignment (away from families), invoice with company details.
    - Retreat: meeting/practice space, flexible meal times, dietary accommodation (vegan/vegetarian), group check-in process, equipment storage.
    - Foodie: kitchen tour, chef meet-and-greet, cooking class option, wine cellar visit, detailed provenance information on menu, Instagram-ready plating.
    - Family (sub-segment of FIT): baby cot, high chair, child-friendly menu, safe play area, connecting rooms, early dinner service.
  - **Retention strategy**: how to bring them back.
    - All segments: post-stay thank-you email with direct booking incentive for next stay.
    - FIT/Foodie/Local: seasonal newsletter (quarterly) announcing new menu, events, and packages. Direct booking discount (10% or value-add).
    - Corporate: annual rate review meeting. Proactive outreach before budget season (September–October).
    - Retreat: feedback form to facilitator, offer first-right-of-refusal for same dates next year.

### Pas 5: Set Segment Mix Targets — Portfolio Management
- Define the ideal revenue mix by segment for the year. This is a strategic decision, not a passive observation.
- Example mix targets for a boutique rural property with culinary concept:
  - FIT (Direct + OTA combined): 45% of room nights (target: shift Direct share from 20% to 30% within this).
  - Foodie/Gastro: 15% (highest per-night value when F&B is included).
  - Corporate/MICE: 15% (fills midweek, shoulder/low season).
  - Retreat: 10% (high-value, fills multi-night blocks).
  - Local/Staycation: 10% (weekend filler, high repeat).
  - Long Stay: 3% (opportunistic, low-season filler).
  - Promo/Deal: 2% (minimal, only for distressed inventory).
- **Seasonal mix adjustment**: the mix should shift by season because demand composition changes:
  - Summer (Peak): maximize FIT and Foodie (highest ADR, strong organic demand). Reduce or eliminate promotional rates.
  - Shoulder (Spring/Autumn): grow Corporate, Retreat, and Local (reliable demand that does not exist organically). Activate event-driven packages.
  - Winter (Low): prioritize Corporate retreats, Long Stay, Local weekend escapes, and themed events (Christmas market dinner, New Year's Eve). Accept a higher proportion of promotional bookings to cover fixed costs.
- **Monitor actual mix vs. target** monthly in the KPI dashboard (HM-010). If a high-value segment is underperforming, trigger a dedicated acquisition campaign:
  - Corporate below target → sales blitz: call 20 companies, send corporate rate proposal.
  - Retreat below target → outreach to 10 retreat facilitators on BookRetreats.com, offer a familiarization visit.
  - Foodie below target → invest in food media content, invite a food blogger for a review stay.

### Pas 6: Segment-Specific Marketing Metrics
- Track acquisition effectiveness per segment:
  - **Cost per Acquisition (CPA)**: total marketing spend for the segment / number of bookings from that segment. Include OTA commission as a marketing cost for OTA segments.
  - **Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI)**: net revenue from segment / marketing spend for segment. Target ROMI > 5:1 for organic segments, > 3:1 for paid acquisition segments.
  - **Conversion rate by channel**: for direct website, track visitors → booking conversion rate by segment (use UTM parameters in marketing links). Industry average for hotel direct websites: 2–4% conversion rate. Below 2% suggests a website usability or pricing problem.
- Use these metrics to allocate marketing budget to the highest-ROMI segments. A segment with 8:1 ROMI deserves more budget than a segment with 2:1 ROMI, even if the second segment is larger.

### Pas 7: Annual Segment Review — Strategic Reset
- Once per year (October/November, before budget season): re-run the full segmentation analysis with the latest 12 months of data.
- Key questions for the review:
  - Have segment rankings changed? Is a segment that was #4 last year now #2? This may indicate a market shift that requires strategic reallocation.
  - Are any new segments emerging? Examples: "digital nomad" segment appeared post-2020; "bleisure" (business + leisure) is growing. If a new segment represents >5% of bookings without any targeted effort, it deserves formal recognition and strategy.
  - Which segments are declining? If a segment lost >20% volume year-over-year, investigate: is it a property problem (service gap) or a market shift (the segment is disappearing)?
  - Is the channel mix within each segment healthy? If 80% of FIT bookings come from a single OTA, your revenue depends on that OTA's algorithm — a dangerous dependency.
- Update the Segment Strategy Matrix with new channel, rate, service, and retention strategies based on the review findings.
- Present the updated segmentation to the ownership/management team as part of the annual budget process (HM-013). Segment strategy drives revenue targets, which drive the budget.

### Pas 8: Micro-Segmentation for Personalization
- Beyond the primary segments, identify micro-segments that enable service personalization:
  - **Anniversary/Birthday travelers**: flag from reservation notes and past stay data. Trigger surprise amenity (see HM-017).
  - **Wine enthusiasts**: identified from F&B purchase history (ordered premium wine, attended wine tasting). Target with vineyard tour packages and wine dinner invitations.
  - **Outdoor/Active travelers**: identified from activity bookings or profile preferences. Target with hiking packages, cycling routes, seasonal nature experiences.
  - **Photographers/Content creators**: identified from social media check-in tags or reservation notes mentioning photography. Offer golden-hour photo opportunities, scenic room assignments, and permission to use their content (in exchange for a future discount).
- Micro-segments do not require separate marketing campaigns — they require service personalization during the stay and targeted offers in post-stay communication. A guest who receives a wine recommendation based on their last visit's purchase history feels genuinely known — this is the loyalty mechanism that no chain hotel can replicate.

## Note
- **The most dangerous trap**: optimizing only for the largest segment. Your largest segment (typically FIT-OTA) often has the lowest net ADR due to commission. The second or third largest segment may have 30–50% higher net ADR and better retention. Portfolio thinking — not volume thinking — drives profitability.
- **Common mistake — treating all OTA guests as one segment**: a Booking.com Genius guest and a Booking.com standard guest have different behaviors. Genius guests are repeat Booking.com users (high travel frequency, strong review behavior) but hard to convert to direct. Standard OTA guests are more convertible. Separate them if your data allows.
- **Edge case — segment overlap**: a guest may fit multiple segments (a corporate traveler who is also a foodie, a local guest who books for a retreat). Assign to the segment that best describes their primary booking motivation. If unclear, default to the segment with the highest strategic value.
- **Albastru / Șapte Focuri context**: the Foodie/Gastro-tourist and Local/Staycation segments are likely the most underexploited opportunities. The fire-kitchen concept is a powerful magnet for food-motivated travelers, but they need to discover you — invest in food media visibility (Instagram food content, food blogger outreach, Google My Business restaurant listing with professional photos). The Local segment is underexploited because locals may not know the property offers accommodation — they see it as "that restaurant with the fire." Build a dedicated "Dinner + Stay" package targeting this segment: dinner at Sapte Focuri + overnight in the best available room + breakfast = a complete experience priced 20% below the sum of individual components. Promote through local Facebook groups, Instagram stories, and partnerships with local businesses. The corporate retreat segment is the single biggest opportunity for midweek/shoulder season revenue — Pitesti, Bucharest corporate offices, and the 107+ IT companies identified in the Albastru outreach list are all potential sources. A "Team Retreat at Sapte Focuri" package (meeting space + lunch + team cooking experience + accommodation + dinner) can command 500–800 RON per person per night.
