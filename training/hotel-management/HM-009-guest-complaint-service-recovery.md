# Guest Complaint Handling and Service Recovery

## Obiectiv
Turn every guest complaint into a loyalty-building moment using a structured service recovery protocol — targeting a recovery satisfaction rate of 85%+ (guest rates the recovery positively), maintaining an online review score of 8.5+ on Booking.com and 4.5+ on Google, and converting 30%+ of complaining guests into repeat bookers through exceptional recovery execution.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Complaint Reception — The LEARN Framework
- Every staff member who interacts with guests must know the LEARN framework. It is the universal first-response protocol regardless of complaint type or severity:
  - **L — Listen**: give the guest your full, uninterrupted attention. Face them. Make eye contact. Put down your phone, step away from the computer. Do not interrupt. Do not formulate your response while they are still speaking. Let them finish completely. If the guest is emotional, let them vent — interrupting an emotional guest escalates the situation.
  - **E — Empathize**: acknowledge the emotion BEFORE addressing the problem. The guest needs to feel heard before they can hear your solution. "I completely understand how frustrating that must have been." "I can see why that would be upsetting — I would feel the same way." Use empathy phrases, not defensiveness. Never say "That's not usually a problem" or "Nobody else has complained about that."
  - **A — Apologize**: apologize for the guest's experience — not necessarily for fault (which may be disputed or unclear), but for the fact that they did not have the experience they expected. "I sincerely apologize that your room was not up to standard when you arrived." The apology must be genuine — a formulaic "sorry for the inconvenience" is worse than no apology because it sounds dismissive.
  - **R — Respond**: provide a specific, actionable solution. Not "I'll look into it" (vague and weak) but "I'm going to move you to a clean room right now, and I'll have a complimentary bottle of wine sent up while we get everything perfect for you" (specific and immediate). The guest should leave the interaction knowing exactly what will happen and by when.
  - **N — Notify**: tell the guest when they will hear back and who will follow up. "I'll personally check in with you at 16:00 to make sure everything is resolved." NEVER miss this follow-up. The follow-up is where recovery either succeeds or fails. A resolution without follow-up leaves the guest uncertain.
- **Body language matters**: stand or sit at the same level as the guest (do not stand behind a high desk looking down). Uncross your arms. Lean slightly forward. Nod when they speak. These signals communicate "I am taking you seriously."
- **Escalation language**: if the agent cannot resolve the complaint at their level, use escalation language that reassures the guest: "I want to make sure this is handled perfectly for you. Let me bring in my manager — they have more authority to make this right."

### Pas 2: Classify Complaint Severity — The 3-Level System
- **Level 1 — Operational (resolve within 30 minutes)**:
  - Examples: noise complaint, room too hot/cold, missing amenity (towels, toiletries), wrong item on bill, slow service, Wi-Fi not working, television not working.
  - Handler: front desk agent or department supervisor.
  - Resolution: fix the issue immediately. No compensation required for most Level 1 complaints — just quick, cheerful resolution. If the resolution takes more than 15 minutes, offer a gesture of goodwill (complimentary beverage, fresh amenity kit).
  - Documentation: log in the shift handover book. No formal complaint record unless the issue recurs.
- **Level 2 — Service Failure (resolve within 2 hours, Duty Manager involved)**:
  - Examples: room was dirty on arrival, reserved room type not available (must downgrade or relocate), facility not working (shower, AC, elevator), F&B quality significantly below standard, significant wait at check-in (>15 minutes), lost reservation.
  - Handler: Duty Manager, supported by the department head.
  - Resolution: fix the issue + offer compensation proportional to the severity (see Pas 4). A dirty room on arrival requires a room change, immediate clean, AND a goodwill gesture. Simply cleaning the room is not sufficient — the trust is already broken.
  - Documentation: formal entry in the Guest Complaint Log. Follow-up required within 2 hours.
- **Level 3 — Serious (GM involved within 1 hour, incident report filed)**:
  - Examples: safety incident (injury on property, fire, flood in room), harassment by staff, discriminatory treatment, significant health issue related to property (foodborne illness, allergic reaction from undisclosed allergen), theft of guest property, privacy breach (unauthorized room entry).
  - Handler: GM or property owner personally. Legal review if potential liability.
  - Resolution: immediate action to ensure guest safety and well-being. Full documentation. Medical assistance if required (call 112). Compensation at GM's discretion (may include full refund, complimentary future stays, and formal apology letter). Legal counsel if the incident involves potential litigation.
  - Documentation: formal Incident Report (separate from Guest Complaint Log). Photos if relevant. Witness statements. Retain for minimum 5 years (statute of limitations for civil claims in Romania).
- **Classification is made at first contact.** If in doubt, escalate one level higher. Under-classifying a serious complaint is far more dangerous than over-classifying a minor one.

### Pas 3: Service Recovery Actions by Complaint Type
- **Room cleanliness complaint**: (1) Apologize sincerely. (2) Offer immediate room change to a clean, inspected room — ideally an upgrade if available (the upgrade signals you take the complaint seriously). (3) If no alternative room is available, have housekeeping deep-clean the current room within 30 minutes while the guest waits in the restaurant with a complimentary beverage. (4) Send a goodwill amenity to the room (wine, fruit plate, local specialty). (5) Follow up within 2 hours. (6) Document and investigate: why did the room pass inspection in a dirty state?
- **Noise complaint**: (1) Acknowledge and apologize. (2) If noise is from another guest: call the noisy room and request they reduce noise. If they do not comply within 15 minutes, the Duty Manager visits in person. (3) If noise is structural (thin walls, proximity to road/bar): offer room change to a quieter room. (4) Provide earplugs as an interim measure. (5) If the noise disrupted sleep significantly, consider a partial room charge reduction (10–20%).
- **Billing dispute**: (1) Never argue. (2) Print the folio. (3) Walk through each charge with the guest. (4) If the charge is correct but the guest disputes it: waive it. The cost of one disputed charge (typically 20–100 RON) is infinitely cheaper than the cost of a negative review. (5) If the charge is incorrect: correct immediately, apologize for the error, and explain what happened.
- **F&B complaint** (wrong dish, poor quality, long wait): (1) Replace the dish immediately at no charge. (2) The F&B manager or senior server personally apologizes to the table. (3) If the meal was significantly below standard, consider voiding the affected dish from the bill. (4) If the entire dining experience was unsatisfactory, consider voiding the full F&B charge and inviting the guest to return for a complimentary dinner.
- **Delayed check-in / room not ready**: (1) Apologize for the delay. (2) Store luggage securely. (3) Invite to the restaurant or bar for a complimentary welcome beverage. (4) Provide a specific, realistic time when the room will be ready (confirm with housekeeping before committing). (5) When the room is ready, personally escort the guest. (6) If the delay exceeds 2 hours past advertised check-in time, offer a gesture of goodwill.
- **Overbooking / must walk a guest**: see HM-001 Pas 6. This is a Level 2 complaint by default.
- **Safety incident**: see Level 3 protocol. Guest safety is the absolute priority — all other considerations (cost, reputation, inconvenience) are secondary.

### Pas 4: Compensation Authority Matrix
- Empower your team to resolve complaints without unnecessary escalation. Staff who must say "Let me check with my manager" and then disappear for 10 minutes make the guest feel unimportant and frustrated.
- **Front Desk Agent** (Level 1 authority):
  - Waive charges up to 100 RON.
  - Offer a complimentary F&B item up to 50 RON value (dessert, drink, breakfast).
  - Move a guest to a different room of the same category.
  - Provide immediate amenity replacement (extra towels, pillows, toiletries).
  - Offer a late checkout at no charge (if room is not needed for same-day arrival).
- **Duty Manager / Department Supervisor** (Level 2 authority):
  - Waive charges up to 500 RON.
  - Offer a room upgrade at no charge.
  - Offer a complimentary meal (up to 200 RON value).
  - Offer a late checkout, early check-in, or LOS extension at reduced rate.
  - Authorize a 10–20% room rate discount for the affected night.
- **Front Office Manager / F&B Manager** (Level 2+ authority):
  - Waive charges up to 1,500 RON.
  - Offer 1 complimentary night.
  - Offer F&B credit up to 500 RON.
  - Issue a formal written apology.
  - Authorize a 50% room rate discount for the full stay (exceptional cases).
- **GM / Property Owner** (Level 3 authority):
  - Waive any charge.
  - Offer a full complimentary stay.
  - Issue a formal apology letter on property letterhead.
  - Authorize full refund.
  - Involve legal counsel if required.
- **Print the Compensation Authority Matrix on a laminated card** and place one at each front desk station and one in the Duty Manager's office. Train every staff member on their authority level during onboarding. Empowered staff resolve complaints faster and more effectively.

### Pas 5: Log, Track, and Follow Up
- Every Level 2+ complaint is logged in the **Guest Complaint Log** (Google Sheets, accessible to Duty Manager, FOM, and GM):
  - Columns: Date | Time | Guest Name | Room | Complaint Description | Severity Level | Handler | Resolution Offered | Compensation Authorized | Follow-Up Time | Follow-Up Completed (Y/N) | Guest Satisfaction After Recovery (Satisfied/Neutral/Dissatisfied) | Root Cause | Corrective Action.
- **Follow-up is mandatory and timed**: the agent who handled the complaint personally follows up with the guest within the promised timeframe (Pas 1, N step). The follow-up conversation:
  - "Mr. Ionescu, I wanted to check in — is everything to your satisfaction now? Is there anything else I can do?"
  - If the guest is satisfied: "I'm so glad we could make it right. Please don't hesitate to contact me directly if anything else comes up during your stay."
  - If the guest is still dissatisfied: escalate to the next authority level immediately. A complaint that requires a second recovery attempt should be handled by a more senior staff member.
- **Post-resolution touchpoint**: for Level 2+ complaints, the GM or Duty Manager should make a personal appearance before the guest's departure. This signals organizational seriousness. "Mr. Ionescu, I understand you had an issue with your room yesterday. I wanted to personally apologize and make sure the rest of your stay has been exceptional."

### Pas 6: Online Review Response Protocol
- **Monitor daily**: check Booking.com, Google Reviews, TripAdvisor, and Airbnb reviews every morning. Set up email notifications for new reviews on each platform.
- **Respond to ALL reviews within 48 hours** — positive and negative. Response rate is a ranking factor on all platforms.
- **Positive review response** (30 seconds, but essential):
  - Thank the guest by name (if visible).
  - Reference a specific detail from their review: "We're so glad you enjoyed the fire-kitchen dinner — Chef [name] will be thrilled to hear that."
  - Invite them back: "We hope to welcome you again soon!"
  - Do NOT use generic templates — guests and future readers can tell.
- **Negative review response** (the most important marketing writing you will do):
  1. Thank the guest for taking the time to share their feedback.
  2. Acknowledge the specific issue — do not be vague. "We're sorry the room was not up to standard when you arrived."
  3. Apologize sincerely without making excuses.
  4. Describe the corrective action taken: "We've since increased our room inspection frequency to prevent this from happening again."
  5. Offer to continue the conversation privately: "Please reach out to us at [email] — we'd love the opportunity to make it right."
  6. Invite them back: "We hope to have the chance to show you the experience we strive for."
- **What NEVER to do in a review response**:
  - Never be defensive. "That's not true" or "This never happened" makes you look combative.
  - Never blame the guest. Even if the guest is wrong, future readers will side with the guest.
  - Never disclose private details about the stay ("But we gave you a free upgrade...") — this violates trust and is potentially a GDPR issue.
  - Never use sarcasm or passive aggression. "We're sorry you feel that way" is condescending.
  - Never write an essay. 4–5 sentences maximum. Long responses look defensive.
- **The audience is not the reviewer — it is the next 1,000 potential guests who read the review and your response.** A calm, professional, empathetic response to a negative review actually increases booking confidence.

### Pas 7: Root Cause Analysis and Systemic Prevention
- **Weekly**: review the Guest Complaint Log for patterns. If the same room, same issue, or same department appears repeatedly:
  - Same room with noise complaints 3 weeks in a row → it is a structural issue (location near a noise source). Reassign the room to less noise-sensitive segments or invest in soundproofing.
  - Cleanliness complaints from multiple guests → it is a housekeeping process issue. Re-train the team, increase inspection frequency (HM-002), or investigate whether the attendant assignment load is too high.
  - F&B complaints clustering on a specific shift → it is a staffing or management issue on that shift.
- **Monthly report**: present the top 5 complaint categories by volume to department heads. Format: Category | Count | Average Resolution Time | Average Compensation Cost | Root Cause | Corrective Action Assigned | Deadline. Each department head is responsible for presenting their corrective action at the next meeting.
- **Review score tracking**: track Booking.com review score (target: >8.5), Google rating (target: >4.5), and response rate (target: 100% within 48 hours). If the review score drops by 0.2+ points in a single month, trigger an immediate service quality investigation — identify what changed (new staff? changed process? maintenance issue?) and correct.
- **The complaint iceberg**: research shows that for every guest who complains, 26 others are dissatisfied but say nothing — they simply do not return. A low complaint volume is NOT evidence of high satisfaction. Actively solicit feedback (mid-stay touchpoint per HM-017, post-stay survey) to surface issues that silent guests would not mention.

### Pas 8: Proactive Complaint Prevention
- The best complaint is the one that never happens. Invest in prevention:
  - **Pre-arrival expectation setting**: clear, honest descriptions on OTA and website. Photos that represent reality (not over-edited hero shots of a different property). If the road to the property is unpaved, mention it in the directions. If the Wi-Fi is limited, state it. Guests tolerate imperfections but not surprises.
  - **Mid-stay touchpoint** (see HM-017): the single most powerful prevention tool. A brief check during the second morning catches 80% of issues before they become departure complaints or reviews. "Good morning, Mr. Ionescu — is everything to your liking? Anything we can improve?"
  - **Maintenance prevention**: a dripping faucet, a squeaky door, a flickering light — these are not emergencies, but they accumulate into a "the property is not well-maintained" perception. Run a monthly room-by-room maintenance walkthrough and fix every small issue proactively.
  - **Staff training refresh**: every quarter, run a 30-minute complaint handling refresher with role-play scenarios. Rotate scenarios based on recent actual complaints from the log.
  - **Mystery guest**: once per year, arrange for a trusted contact to stay as an anonymous guest and provide a detailed evaluation of every touchpoint. This reveals blind spots that internal assessment misses.

### Pas 9: Turning Recovery into Loyalty
- A guest whose complaint was perfectly resolved often rates their stay HIGHER than a guest who had no complaint. This is the **Service Recovery Paradox** — and it is your most powerful loyalty mechanism if you execute it consistently.
- **Post-recovery loyalty actions**:
  - After a successfully resolved Level 2+ complaint: send a personal email from the GM within 48 hours of checkout. "Dear Mr. Ionescu, I wanted to personally thank you for bringing the issue to our attention and for giving us the chance to make it right. Your feedback helps us improve, and I hope the rest of your stay was enjoyable. As a gesture of appreciation, I'd like to offer you [10% discount / free dinner / upgrade on your next stay]. I hope we can welcome you back."
  - Flag the guest's profile in PMS as "Recovery Guest — extra attention on return." On their next stay, the front desk agent should acknowledge the recovery: "Welcome back, Mr. Ionescu — we're so happy to see you again. We've prepared [special touch] for you."
  - Track the repeat rate of recovery guests vs. complaint-free guests. If your recovery process is strong, recovery guests should have an equal or higher repeat rate.

## Note
- **The Service Recovery Paradox is real**: guests whose complaints were perfectly resolved report higher satisfaction than guests who had no complaint. This means complaints are not threats — they are opportunities. But the paradox only works if the recovery is genuinely excellent. A mediocre recovery (slow, impersonal, insufficient) makes the situation worse than not recovering at all.
- **Common mistake — offering compensation before solving the problem**: solve the problem first, then offer compensation as a goodwill gesture. Compensation without resolution is seen as "buying the guest off" and creates resentment.
- **Common mistake — defensive escalation**: "Let me get my manager" said in a flustered tone signals that you cannot handle the situation. "I'd like to bring in someone with more authority to ensure this is resolved perfectly for you" signals that you take it seriously.
- **Edge case — the professional complainer**: some guests complain strategically to extract free services. Signs: vague complaints, refusal to accept reasonable solutions, immediate demand for compensation, mention of negative review as leverage. Handle these guests per the same LEARN framework (do not lower your standard), resolve reasonably, but do not over-compensate. Document thoroughly. If a pattern emerges across multiple stays, the GM may choose to decline future bookings.
- **Edge case — complaint about something you cannot fix** (e.g., weather, road conditions, lack of nearby attractions): empathize genuinely, offer alternative suggestions (indoor activities, restaurant recommendation), and provide a small gesture of goodwill. Do not apologize for things outside your control — instead, express shared disappointment: "I know, the weather has been disappointing today. Can I arrange [alternative activity] for you?"
- **Albastru / Sapte Focuri context**: for a boutique property with 6–10 rooms, the GM or property owner often handles Level 2+ complaints personally. This is a significant competitive advantage — personal involvement from the owner is the most powerful recovery tool available. Use it deliberately: when a complaint is serious, the owner's personal intervention ("I'm [name], the owner of this property, and I want to personally make sure this is resolved for you") communicates a level of care that no chain hotel can match. The review response should also reflect this personal ownership: sign responses with the owner's name, not "Management Team." For a property built on authenticity and personal hospitality, the complaint recovery process is a direct expression of the brand promise.
