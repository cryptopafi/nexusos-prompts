# F&B Service Operations SOP

## Obiectiv
Deliver a consistent, revenue-optimized food and beverage service experience from mise en place through closing — targeting an average spend per cover of BAR+30% above food cost, a food cost percentage of 28–32%, zero allergy incidents, and a guest satisfaction score of 9.0+ on F&B-related review categories. The F&B operation is both a profit center and the primary experiential differentiator for properties with a culinary concept.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Pre-Service Setup (Mise en Place) — Foundation of Every Service
- Receive the day's reservation list from front office by 07:30 (breakfast), 11:00 (lunch), 16:00 (dinner). Cross-reference with the in-house guest list — all in-house guests are potential covers even if they have not reserved. For a 10-room property at 80% occupancy, expect 16 guests for breakfast (near-100% attendance if breakfast is included) and 8–12 for dinner (50–70% dining-in rate for rural properties with limited nearby alternatives).
- Calculate covers needed: reservations + walk-in buffer (10–15% of capacity for urban, 20–30% for destination properties where restaurant is open to external guests) + in-house guest estimate. Communicate the cover count to the kitchen by the deadline — the kitchen orders and preps based on this number.
- Table setup standard by tier:
  - **Casual/breakfast**: clean surface, placemat or cloth, cutlery set (fork, knife, spoon), water glass, napkin, menu card or buffet guide.
  - **Semi-formal dinner**: pressed linen tablecloth (check for stains under strong light before laying), base plate or charger plate, full cutlery set (starter fork/knife outermost, main fork/knife inner, dessert spoon above plate), wine glass + water glass above knife, cloth napkin folded (bishop's hat or pyramid fold), candle or table lamp (if evening service), fresh flower or seasonal decoration (never artificial flowers in a property that claims authenticity).
  - **Fine dining / tasting menu**: as above plus amuse-bouche spoon, bread plate with butter knife at 10 o'clock position, second wine glass for pairing, menu card personalized with guest name for events.
- Check and replenish all service stations: condiment sets (salt, pepper, sugar, oil, vinegar — all containers clean and full), bread baskets with bread wraps, butter portions at correct temperature (10–12 degrees C, not fridge-cold), water carafes chilled.
- Brief all service staff (mandatory, 10 minutes before service opens):
  - Today's specials: full description including key ingredients, preparation method, and suggested wine pairing. Every server must taste the daily special before service — you cannot sell what you have not tasted.
  - Sold-out items: list items that are unavailable and alternatives to suggest.
  - VIP tables: which guests require recognition (by name, special occasion, dietary needs).
  - Allergy flags: any pre-communicated dietary restrictions from reservations or guest profiles. Assign allergy tables to the most experienced server.
  - Service timing targets: amuse-bouche within 5 minutes of seating, starter delivery within 15 minutes of order, starter-to-main gap 15–20 minutes, main-to-dessert gap 12–15 minutes, total dining experience 75–90 minutes for a 3-course dinner.
  - Revenue target for the service: "Tonight we have 28 covers booked. Our target average spend is 180 RON per cover, which means we need to achieve 5,040 RON total. That means every table should hear about the tasting menu and the wine pairing."
- Polish all glassware, cutlery, and service equipment. Hold each glass up to a light source — zero watermarks, fingerprints, or lipstick residue. A single dirty glass undermines the entire table setting.

### Pas 2: Guest Seating and Order Taking
- Greet arriving guests within 30 seconds of entering the restaurant. For in-house hotel guests: "Good evening, Mr. Ionescu — welcome to Șapte Focuri. We have your table ready by the window." For external guests: "Good evening, welcome to our restaurant. Do you have a reservation?" Never leave a guest standing at the entrance looking for direction.
- Seat guests and present menus. Pour water immediately — a guest with water in hand feels attended to; a guest with an empty table feels forgotten.
- Allow 3–5 minutes for menu review. Watch for the "closed menu" signal (menus placed flat on the table) — approach to take orders within 60 seconds of this signal.
- Suggestive selling — the most impactful revenue lever in F&B:
  - **Aperitif**: "May I bring you something to drink while you look at the menu? Our house spritz with elderflower cordial is lovely." (Average aperitif order increases per-cover spend by 15–20 RON.)
  - **Starters**: "I'd highly recommend the smoked trout — it's prepared right here on our open fire and the flavour is incredible." Lead with enthusiasm, not price.
  - **Main course**: if the guest is undecided, recommend the signature dish or chef's special: "Our signature dish is the lamb cooked in the clay pot over the fire for 6 hours — it falls apart on the fork. It's what most guests come back for."
  - **Wine pairing**: "We have a beautiful Feteasca Neagra from Dealu Mare that pairs perfectly with the lamb. Shall I bring a bottle, or would you prefer to try it by the glass first?" Offering by-the-glass reduces resistance — many guests who start with a glass end up ordering the bottle.
  - **Dessert and coffee**: address in Pas 3.
- Record orders with precision: table number, seat position (position 1 = guest nearest to the kitchen door, number clockwise), item name with full modifications (no onion, sauce on the side, medium-rare, gluten-free bread). Use position numbering so food is served to the correct guest without asking "Who ordered the lamb?" — asking is a service failure.
- Repeat the complete order back to the guest: "So that's the smoked trout and the mici platter to start, followed by the clay pot lamb and the grilled mountain trout, with a bottle of the Feteasca Neagra. Is that everything?"
- Enter the order into the POS system immediately. Kitchen printer produces the docket with table, position, items, and modifications. Allergy orders must be flagged with a red docket marker or a digital alert — kitchen treats allergy tickets as the highest priority.

### Pas 3: Food and Beverage Service Standards
- **Plate handling**: food is served from the guest's left (because most people are right-handed and their dominant hand stays free); cleared from the right. Beverages are served from the right. Never reach across a guest.
- **Temperature control**: all hot food served hot. Plate temperature check: the plate should be warm to the touch but not burning (65–70 degrees C for the food, plate preheated to 50–55 degrees C). Cold dishes served at refrigerator temperature (4–6 degrees C). Food that arrives at table at the wrong temperature must be returned to the kitchen immediately — never serve compromised food.
- **Presentation**: before delivering, the expeditor (or server at smaller properties) checks every plate against the menu photo or plating standard. Plate must be clean-rimmed (no sauce drips), garnish fresh and intentional, portion size per standard. If the plate does not meet standard, return to kitchen before it reaches the table. Guests do not see the kitchen's pressure — they only see the plate.
- **Check-back**: 2 minutes after main course delivery, the section server approaches: "Is everything to your liking? How is the lamb?" This is not a formality — listen for genuine feedback. If the guest says "it's fine" without enthusiasm, probe gently: "Is the seasoning right for you?" This prevents a mediocre experience from becoming a negative review.
- **Active table management**: clear finished plates promptly but never before all guests at the table are finished (clearing one plate while another guest is still eating pressures the slow eater). Replenish water proactively — never let a water glass sit empty for more than 3 minutes. Offer bread refills after the first basket is half-consumed.
- **Wine service protocol**: present the bottle to the host before opening (label visible). Open at the table. Pour a small tasting amount to the host. Wait for approval. Serve all other guests (ladies first, host last). White and rosé wine: serve from an ice bucket at the table. Red wine: serve at cellar temperature (16–18 degrees C). Refill glasses when approximately one-third remains — do not wait for empty.
- **Dessert and coffee upsell**: when the main course plates are 80% cleared, present the dessert menu: "Can I tempt you with our dessert menu? The chocolate fondant needs 12 minutes to prepare, so I wanted to ask now while you finish." This pre-order technique increases dessert conversion by 20–30% because it removes the wait. Follow dessert with a coffee offer. For a premium experience, offer digestifs: "Would you like a palinca or a herbal liqueur to finish the evening?" Average revenue from dessert + coffee + digestif: 60–90 RON per cover.
- **Table pacing for multi-course service**: communicate pace to the kitchen. If a table is lingering over conversation after starters, send a hold signal to the kitchen (do not fire the main course until the table is cleared and ready). If a table is eating quickly, alert the kitchen to fire the next course promptly. The ideal pace is guest-controlled — the server reads the table, not the clock.

### Pas 4: Allergy and Special Dietary Management
- **Pre-arrival identification**: when taking a reservation, always ask: "Do any guests have food allergies or dietary requirements?" Log in the reservation system with a visual flag. Brief the kitchen and assigned server during the pre-service meeting.
- **At the table**: when the guest identifies an allergy at order time, the server must (1) record it on the order ticket in capital letters with a highlight, (2) inform the kitchen verbally in addition to the docket, (3) verify with the kitchen which menu items are safe and which require modification.
- **Kitchen protocol**: allergy orders are prepared on dedicated surfaces with dedicated utensils. Cross-contamination prevention: separate cutting boards, clean pans (not wiped — clean), clean oil (not shared fryer oil). The kitchen lead must verbally confirm to the server: "Table 4, position 2 — lamb is allergen-free, prepared on a clean station."
- **Delivery**: allergy dishes are delivered by the section server or the F&B supervisor — never by a runner who may not know the context. The server verbally confirms with the guest at delivery: "This is your lamb, Mr. Ionescu, prepared without any nuts as you requested."
- **The 14 EU allergens**: all service staff must know the 14 mandatory declaration allergens (cereals containing gluten, crustaceans, eggs, fish, peanuts, soybeans, milk, nuts, celery, mustard, sesame, sulphites, lupin, molluscs). Allergen information for every dish must be documented and available (either on the menu, in a separate allergen matrix, or available on request).
- **If in doubt, do NOT serve**: if there is any uncertainty about whether a dish contains an allergen, return to the kitchen and confirm with the chef who prepared it. Never guess. An anaphylactic reaction is a life-threatening emergency and a potential criminal liability.
- **Emergency protocol**: every service staff member must know the location of the nearest first-aid kit and whether the property stocks epinephrine auto-injectors (EpiPen). If a guest shows signs of an allergic reaction: (1) call 112 immediately, (2) administer EpiPen if available and trained, (3) keep the guest comfortable, (4) do NOT move them if they are having difficulty breathing, (5) document the incident in the Incident Report.

### Pas 5: Bill Presentation and Payment
- Present the bill only when the guest signals for it (makes eye contact, waves, or verbally requests). Never leave the bill unsolicited on the table — it signals "please leave" and is hostile to the hospitality experience. Exception: at breakfast/lunch where turn-time matters and guests have finished, a gentle "Shall I prepare your bill whenever you're ready?" is acceptable.
- Bill format: itemized, clear, printed from POS. Every item named clearly (not internal codes). VAT shown separately per Romanian fiscal requirements. If the property issues fiscal receipts from a certified POS, ensure the fiscal receipt accompanies the bill.
- Payment methods accepted: cash (RON), card (Visa/Mastercard/Amex — confirm which), room charge (for hotel guests), vouchers (gift cards, event credits). For room charges: verify the guest's name and room number, have them sign the charge slip, submit the signed slip to front office by end of service — unsigned room charges are revenue leakage risk.
- **Tipping culture in Romania**: tips are not included in the bill by default. Do not add a mandatory service charge unless it is clearly communicated at booking and on the menu. Tip pooling vs. individual tips is a property policy decision — document and communicate it to all staff during onboarding.
- If a guest disputes a charge: remain calm, apologize for any confusion, review the order record in POS. If the charge is correct but the guest insists, call the F&B supervisor — the supervisor can void up to 100 RON without manager approval (see HM-009 Compensation Matrix). Never argue with a guest over a bill at the table — it ruins the experience for them and for every other table within earshot.
- After payment, thank the guest genuinely. If they are hotel guests, invite them to breakfast or the next evening's service: "We have a special tasting menu tomorrow night — shall I reserve your table?"

### Pas 6: Post-Service Closing and Revenue Reporting
- Run the POS end-of-service report immediately after the last guest departs:
  - Total covers served (vs. forecasted covers — analyze variance).
  - Total F&B revenue by category: food, wine, other beverages, desserts.
  - Average spend per cover (target depends on positioning — for a mid-premium rural property: 120–200 RON per cover for dinner, 40–70 RON for breakfast).
  - Top-selling items and zero-selling items (items with zero sales for 3 consecutive services should be reviewed for menu removal).
  - Revenue per server (to identify upselling performance by individual).
- Reconcile cash payments vs. POS cash totals. Variance of more than 10 RON requires investigation. Cash handling is the highest-risk area for F&B fraud — enforce strict POS discipline (every transaction must be rung through POS before payment is accepted).
- **Food waste log**: record leftover quantities by dish (portions prepared minus portions sold minus portions consumed by staff meal). Calculate waste percentage per dish. Threshold: if a dish consistently returns more than 25% waste (prepped but unsold), the prep quantity needs to be reduced, or the dish needs to be removed/replaced on the menu. Track waste cost weekly — industry target: food waste under 5% of food cost.
- **Staff meal**: designate a staff meal time and menu (typically leftover-appropriate items or a simple dedicated meal). Staff meal cost should be budgeted at 10–15 RON per staff member per shift. This is not a perk — it is an operational necessity. Hungry servers make bad service decisions.
- Reset tables for the next service or next day's breakfast within 20 minutes of the last guest departure.
- Deep clean: all service stations, coffee machine (backflush daily, descale weekly), juice dispensers, bar area, condiment containers, bread warmers. F&B floor mopped. All equipment turned off or set to standby per energy protocol.
- Submit F&B daily report to F&B Manager and Revenue Manager by 23:00. Report includes: covers, revenue, average spend, food cost estimate for the day, waste notes, any service incidents, and tomorrow's reservation count and prep requirements.

### Pas 7: Breakfast Service Specifics (Buffet and Hybrid Models)
- **Buffet setup**: all hot items filled and at temperature by 07:00 (opening time). Temperature check logged at setup, then every 30 minutes: hot items minimum 65 degrees C, cold items maximum 5 degrees C. Use food-safe thermometer — not estimation. Items that fall out of safe temperature range must be replaced, not reheated.
- **Buffet layout design**: follow the natural guest flow — plates at the start, cutlery at the end (so guests can hold the plate with both hands while serving). Logical grouping: cereals/yogurt/fruit first (cold, quick), then bread/pastries, then hot items (eggs, sausage, bacon, traditional items), beverages at the end or at a separate station. Signage: every item labeled with name and allergen symbols. For international guests, label in Romanian and English.
- Replenish buffet items when 30% remaining — do not wait for empty. An empty buffet tray during service signals understaffing and disrespect. One team member should be dedicated to buffet replenishment at all times during peak breakfast hours (07:30–09:30).
- **A la carte items within the buffet model**: eggs to order (scrambled, fried, omelette, poached), fresh juice, and specialty coffee are the items that elevate breakfast from "included" to "experienced." Station a dedicated cook or server to handle these orders within 5 minutes of request.
- **Monitoring guest flow**: during peak hours, ensure a team member is at the restaurant entrance to greet, seat, and pour coffee/juice. The first 60 seconds of the breakfast experience set the tone. If there is a queue, manage it with a wait time estimate and a offered beverage.
- **Waste management at breakfast**: the buffet is the highest-waste meal service. Track food waste by item category after each breakfast. Common over-production items: scrambled eggs (prepare in smaller batches every 20 minutes rather than one large batch), bread (track consumption by day to calibrate daily order), fresh fruit (order 2-day quantities, use FIFO strictly). Target breakfast food cost: 30–35% of breakfast revenue.
- Close breakfast: clear buffet by 10:30. All remaining food either saved for staff meal (if safe temperature maintained) or logged as waste. Clean and reset for lunch by 12:00 (or store setup if no lunch service).

### Pas 8: Event and Private Dining Service
- For private dining events (corporate dinners, celebrations, group tasting menus): assign a dedicated event coordinator from the F&B team from the moment the booking is confirmed.
- Pre-event planning meeting (1 week before): confirm guest count, menu selection, dietary requirements, beverage package (open bar, limited bar, wine pairing only), seating arrangement, AV requirements, timing, and budget.
- Set up the event space 2 hours before arrival. Run a full quality check: table setting complete, correct menu cards, AV tested, music/lighting preset, welcome drinks prepared.
- Service during events follows the same standards as regular service but with tighter coordination. The event coordinator communicates directly with the kitchen on course timing. For a tasting menu: each course fires only when the coordinator signals that the previous course is cleared and the guests are ready.
- Post-event: present the bill to the event organizer (never to individual guests unless pre-agreed). Include a feedback form. Follow up within 48 hours with a thank-you email and an invitation to book the next event.
- **Revenue opportunity**: event and private dining services typically yield the highest per-cover revenue (2–3x regular dinner). For a rural property, position the space for corporate offsites, team retreats, birthday celebrations, and romantic dinners. A dedicated "Fire Kitchen Experience" event format — where guests cook alongside the chef — can command 300–500 RON per person.

### Pas 9: Inventory, Ordering, and Cost Control
- **Weekly F&B inventory**: count all perishable stock (protein, dairy, produce) every Monday morning. Count high-value non-perishables (alcohol, specialty ingredients) weekly. Count all non-perishables monthly.
- **FIFO (First In, First Out)**: all deliveries labeled with date received. Older stock placed at the front of shelves. No exceptions. Expired items are discarded and logged as waste cost.
- **Food cost calculation**: run weekly food cost = (Opening Inventory + Purchases - Closing Inventory) / Food Revenue x 100. Target: 28–32% for a restaurant with fresh, local sourcing. If food cost exceeds 35%, investigate: over-portioning (weigh plated portions against recipe cards), waste (check the waste log), theft (compare inventory movement against sales), or supplier price creep (renegotiate or switch suppliers).
- **Beverage cost**: target 20–28%. Wine by the glass is the highest-margin format — promote it. Cocktails with local spirits (palinca, tuica) can achieve 80%+ margins.
- **Supplier management**: maintain at least 2 approved suppliers for every high-volume category (meat, dairy, produce, beverages) so you are never held hostage by a single supplier. Negotiate quarterly pricing agreements for predictable costs. Prefer local suppliers within 50km — shorter supply chain means fresher product, lower transport cost, and a story to tell guests.
- **Menu engineering**: quarterly, classify every menu item on the Boston Matrix: Stars (high popularity + high margin — promote), Plowhorses (high popularity + low margin — increase price or reduce portion cost), Puzzles (low popularity + high margin — improve visibility on menu), Dogs (low popularity + low margin — remove or replace). Keep the menu compact: 4–6 starters, 6–8 mains, 3–5 desserts. A bloated menu increases waste, slows kitchen execution, and confuses guests.

### Pas 10: Health and Safety Compliance
- **HACCP compliance**: all F&B operations must follow HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) principles. Document critical control points: receiving (check delivery temperatures), storage (cold chain maintenance), preparation (cross-contamination prevention), cooking (core temperature verification — 75 degrees C minimum for poultry, 63 degrees C for beef/lamb), holding (hot food above 65 degrees C, cold below 5 degrees C), serving (time limits for buffet items — maximum 4 hours at safe temperature).
- **Temperature logs**: maintain daily temperature logs for all refrigerators, freezers, and hot-holding equipment. Check twice daily (opening and midday). Any unit outside safe range: investigate immediately, relocate food if necessary, call maintenance.
- **Personal hygiene**: all kitchen and service staff must wash hands upon entering the kitchen, after handling raw food, after using the restroom, after touching face/hair. Handwashing stations must have soap, hot water, and disposable towels (not shared cloth towels).
- **Cleaning schedule**: daily, weekly, and monthly cleaning tasks for all kitchen equipment, surfaces, floors, and storage areas. Documented and signed off by the shift supervisor. External deep cleaning (extraction hood, duct work, grease traps) scheduled quarterly.
- **Pest control**: contract a professional pest control service for monthly inspections. Keep kitchen area sealed (no gaps under doors, screens on windows). Never store food on the floor — all items on shelves minimum 15 cm above ground.

## Note
- **The cardinal sin of F&B service**: making the guest wait without acknowledgment. A guest who waits 10 minutes and is told "Sorry for the wait" feels worse than a guest who waits 10 minutes but was told upfront "Your table will be ready in about 10 minutes — can I bring you a drink at the bar while you wait?"
- **Common mistake — over-describing dishes**: suggestive selling works when it is enthusiastic and brief. A 90-second monologue about each dish makes the guest tune out. One sentence per recommendation, focused on the sensory experience: "The trout is smoked right here over cherry wood — you can taste the fire."
- **Common mistake — ignoring the table after dessert**: post-dessert is the highest-margin moment (coffee, digestif, pralines). Many servers mentally check out after the main course. Train the team that the meal is not over until the guest leaves.
- **Edge case — guest sends food back**: never argue. Apologize, remove the plate, communicate to the kitchen, offer to replace or suggest an alternative. The replacement is complimentary. If the entire meal is unsatisfactory, void the relevant dishes from the bill. A guest who sends food back and is handled gracefully will return; a guest who is made to feel difficult will not.
- **Edge case — large group with split bills**: clarify at the time of ordering whether the bill will be split or shared. If split: enter orders by position from the start. Splitting a shared bill at the end is chaotic and error-prone. For groups of 8+, suggest a pre-agreed set menu to simplify service and billing.
- **Albastru / Șapte Focuri context**: the open-fire kitchen is the primary guest experience differentiator and the single most powerful revenue driver. Every staff member — front desk, housekeeping, F&B — must be trained to describe the fire-cooking technique with genuine enthusiasm. The narrative justifies premium pricing. Specific talking points: "Our chef cooks everything over an open fire using traditional Romanian clay pots called 'tuciuri.' The fire is fed with beech and oak from the local forest. Some dishes cook for 6 hours — you can taste the slow fire in every bite. There is no other restaurant in Romania doing this at this level." This narrative, delivered with authenticity, converts a 150 RON dinner into a 250 RON experience. Cross-sell with accommodation: F&B staff should know the rooms well enough to invite dinner guests to consider staying: "Do you live nearby? We also have beautiful rooms if you'd like to make a weekend of it — and breakfast is included." For the Instagram audience (12K followers), create plating and fire visuals that are inherently shareable. Serve signature dishes with a visible flame element or a dramatic presentation (lifting the clay pot lid at the table to release aromatic steam). Every shareable moment is free marketing.
