# Cornell Guest Experience Framework — Touchpoint Mapping & Loyalty Architecture

## Obiectiv
Design and implement a structured guest experience framework rooted in Cornell School of Hotel Administration methodology — mapping every touchpoint from pre-arrival to post-stay, measuring emotional highs and lows, building loyalty mechanisms that convert one-time visitors into repeat guests, and establishing a feedback loop that continuously improves the experience. Target: 4.8+ overall rating on all OTA platforms and 30%+ repeat guest rate within 18 months.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Guest Journey Mapping — The 7-Stage Touchpoint Audit
- Map every guest interaction across the complete journey. Cornell's framework identifies seven stages: (1) Dreaming/Inspiration, (2) Research & Booking, (3) Pre-Arrival Communication, (4) Arrival & Check-In, (5) In-Stay Experience, (6) Departure & Check-Out, (7) Post-Stay Relationship.
- For each stage, document: the physical or digital touchpoint, the responsible team member, the current quality level (1–5 scale), the emotional target for the guest (e.g., "excitement" at arrival, "comfort" during stay, "gratitude" at departure), and the current gap between target and reality.
- Create a visual Journey Map on a single A3 sheet or digital canvas (Miro, FigJam). Plot the emotional curve — the ideal shape is a "W" with high peaks at arrival, a signature in-stay moment (e.g., the open-fire dinner), and departure, with smaller dips (administrative moments like payment) handled so smoothly they barely register.
- Identify the "Moments of Truth" — the 3–5 interactions that disproportionately determine whether the guest rates 4 or 5 stars. For Albastru/Șapte Focuri, likely candidates: first visual impression of the property, the initial greeting, the first meal cooked on open fire, and the farewell.
- Walk the journey yourself as a guest. Arrive by car on the road guests use, look at what they see first, check in at the desk, enter the room, eat a meal, check out. Take photos and notes at every step. This exercise reveals blind spots that no spreadsheet audit can find.

### Pas 2: Service Blueprint Construction
- Translate the journey map into a Cornell-style Service Blueprint with four layers: (1) Customer Actions (what the guest does), (2) Frontstage Actions (what the guest sees staff doing), (3) Backstage Actions (what happens behind the scenes to deliver the frontstage experience), (4) Support Processes (systems, supplies, infrastructure).
- For each touchpoint, document the "Line of Visibility" — what the guest sees — and the "Line of Internal Interaction" — what happens between departments to make it work. Example: Guest sees "a beautifully set dinner table with candles" (frontstage) → Backstage: housekeeping prepares table linen at 16:00, kitchen preps mise en place by 17:00, host lights candles at 18:30 → Support: linen supplier delivers weekly, candle stock maintained, reservation system triggers table setup.
- Identify fail points — places where the backstage breaks and the guest notices. Common fail points for rural boutique properties: hot water inconsistency, Wi-Fi dead zones, delayed food service when kitchen is overwhelmed, no one at reception when guest arrives unexpectedly.
- For each fail point, design a "Poka-Yoke" (mistake-proofing mechanism): a checklist, a backup system, or a recovery protocol. Example: Wi-Fi dead zone in room 3 → install a mesh extender. Hot water runs out after 4 showers → install a larger boiler or stagger morning check-in times to spread demand.

### Pas 3: Pre-Arrival Experience Design
- Cornell research shows the guest experience begins at booking, not at arrival. Design a pre-arrival communication sequence:
  - **Booking +1 day**: Personalized confirmation email. Include: property story (why Albastru exists, the Șapte Focuri kitchen philosophy), what to expect ("your room overlooks the valley — bring a camera for sunrise"), practical logistics (driving directions with landmarks, parking, nearest ATM).
  - **Arrival -7 days**: Anticipation builder. Local weather forecast, suggest packing (warm layers for evening terrace dining), ask about dietary restrictions and special occasions, offer add-on experiences (cooking class, foraging walk, wine tasting).
  - **Arrival -1 day**: Logistics confirmation. Check-in time, contact number for day-of questions, "We're looking forward to welcoming you tomorrow."
- Each message must feel personal, not automated. Use the guest's name, reference their booking details ("Your room with the fireplace is all ready"), and sign from a real person (the owner, the host).
- Track open rates and response rates. If pre-arrival emails are ignored (open rate <40%), test subject lines, send via WhatsApp instead, or simplify to fewer messages.

### Pas 4: Arrival Experience — The Peak-End Rule Application
- Cornell's guest loyalty module emphasizes the Peak-End Rule (Kahneman): guests remember the emotional peak of their stay and the final moments — everything in between fades. Design the arrival to be the first peak.
- Physical arrival: ensure the approach road and entrance are impeccable — clean signage, trimmed vegetation, visible property name. First impressions are formed in 7 seconds. If guests arrive via a bumpy country road, the transition to a beautiful, well-maintained property entrance must feel like a reward.
- Welcome protocol: personal greeting by name at the car (if possible, the owner). Offer a welcome drink — something local and memorable (homemade elderflower lemonade, fresh apple juice, tuică for the brave). The drink is consumed during a relaxed 5-minute property orientation, not a rushed checklist.
- Room reveal: accompany the guest to the room. Open the door for them. Point out one unique detail ("This table was made by a local craftsman from 200-year-old oak"). Leave them to settle in — do not linger.
- The welcome amenity in the room should tell a story: a handwritten note from the owner, a small basket of local products (jam, honey, dried herbs), a printed card with the evening menu. Cost: 25–50 RON per room. ROI: the Instagram photo of this amenity is worth more than any paid ad.

### Pas 5: In-Stay Experience — Designing the Signature Moment
- Every memorable property has one "Signature Moment" — the experience guests talk about unprompted. For Albastru/Șapte Focuri, the open-fire kitchen dinner is the natural candidate.
- Structure the Signature Moment as an event, not a transaction: guests are invited to watch the cooking, the chef explains what is being prepared, the fire is theatrical and intentional. The meal is served family-style at a communal table if possible. The conversation, the fire, the food, the setting — this is the 5-star review being written in real time.
- Beyond the Signature Moment, design 2–3 smaller "delight surprises" per stay: an unexpected treat at turndown, a hand-drawn map of a hidden walking trail, a spontaneous invitation to the garden to pick herbs for dinner. These should feel unscripted even though they are planned.
- Monitor in-stay satisfaction through light-touch check-ins. One proactive check-in is enough: at breakfast the morning after arrival, ask "How did you sleep? Is everything comfortable?" This is the early warning system for problems — a guest who hesitates here has an issue that can still be fixed.
- Empower every staff member to resolve small issues on the spot without seeking approval. Cornell's framework: any staff member can spend up to 100 RON to fix a guest problem immediately (extra towels, a bottle of wine for an inconvenience, a free dessert). The cost of not resolving in the moment is always higher.

### Pas 6: Departure Design — The End That Shapes Memory
- The Peak-End Rule makes the departure equally important as the arrival. Never let the last interaction be a cold payment transaction at the desk.
- Departure protocol: (1) Prepare the folio the night before, review for accuracy. (2) At checkout, lead with gratitude: "Thank you for spending these days with us." (3) Process payment efficiently — under 3 minutes. (4) Offer a departure gift: a small jar of homemade jam, a sachet of local herbs, a printed recipe from the dinner. Cost: 10–20 RON. (5) Walk the guest to their car. (6) Wave goodbye until they are out of sight.
- The departure gift is the physical anchor of the memory. When the guest opens their suitcase at home and finds the jar of jam, the entire stay rushes back. This is the moment they write the review.
- Plant the review seed naturally during the farewell: "It would mean so much to us if you could share your experience on Google or Booking — it really helps small properties like ours." Do not send a generic automated email — if you must follow up digitally, make it personal and send it 24 hours after departure.

### Pas 7: Post-Stay Loyalty Architecture
- Cornell's guest loyalty research identifies four loyalty levels: (1) Cognitive (aware of you), (2) Affective (feels positive about you), (3) Conative (intends to return), (4) Action (actually books again). Most properties focus only on level 1 and wonder why guests don't return. The framework targets level 4.
- Post-departure sequence:
  - **Departure +1 day**: Personal thank-you message (WhatsApp or email) with 1–2 professional photos from their stay (the dinner table, the view from their room). "We loved having you — here are a few photos from your visit."
  - **Departure +14 days**: Share a piece of content that reconnects them to the experience — a recipe from the dinner they had, a seasonal update ("The vineyard is in full bloom this week"), a story from the property.
  - **Departure +3 months**: Soft re-engagement. "Spring is arriving in Argeș — the wildflowers are starting. If you'd like to visit again, we have a 15% returning guest rate."
  - **Annual**: Birthday or anniversary greeting if you have the date. Seasonal opening announcement.
- Create a Guest Profile Database: name, contact, stay dates, room preference, dietary restrictions, special occasions, notes on what they loved. A simple Google Sheet works for <100 guests/year. This database is the foundation of personalized re-engagement.
- Track the repeat guest rate quarterly. Industry benchmark for boutique/rural: 15–25%. Target for a well-executed loyalty program: 30%+. Each repeat guest costs near-zero to acquire — they are the most profitable segment.

### Pas 8: Measurement & Continuous Improvement Cycle
- Establish a Guest Experience Scorecard with monthly tracking:
  - **NPS (Net Promoter Score)**: survey departing guests with the single question "How likely are you to recommend us to a friend?" (0–10). Target: NPS > 70.
  - **OTA Rating**: average across Booking.com, Google, Airbnb. Target: 4.8+.
  - **Repeat Rate**: returning guests as a % of total guests. Target: 30%.
  - **Pre-Arrival Email Engagement**: open rate (target >50%) and response rate (target >20%).
  - **Upsell/Experience Attach Rate**: % of guests who book an add-on experience. Target: 25%.
  - **Review Generation Rate**: % of departing guests who leave a review on any platform. Target: 40%.
- Conduct a monthly "Experience Review" meeting (30 minutes): read every review from the past month (positive and negative), identify patterns, assign improvements, and celebrate wins. This meeting replaces vague "we should improve" conversations with data-driven action.
- Quarterly: repeat the guest journey walk-through from Step 1. The experience degrades over time without deliberate maintenance — what was excellent 6 months ago may now be merely adequate.

### Pas 9: Staff Training — Embedding the Framework
- The guest experience framework only works if every team member understands and lives it. Conduct a half-day training session covering: the 7-stage journey map, the Peak-End Rule, the Signature Moment philosophy, the empowerment budget (100 RON per staff member to fix issues), and the departure protocol.
- Role-play the key touchpoints: arrival greeting, the in-stay check-in, handling a complaint, the farewell. Practice until it feels natural, not scripted.
- Create a one-page "Experience Cheat Sheet" posted in the staff area with: the 5 Moments of Truth, the empowerment budget amount, the review seed phrase, and the emergency escalation path.
- Monthly: share guest feedback with the team. Read the best review aloud and acknowledge the team members responsible. Read the worst review and discuss what went wrong without blame — focus on system fixes, not individual failures.

## Note
- **Common mistake — over-engineering the sequence**: sending 5 emails before arrival alienates guests who just want to show up and relax. Two messages before arrival is the maximum for a boutique property. Read the guest — some want detailed communication, others want peace. Adapt.
- **Common mistake — copying luxury hotel scripts**: phrases like "Is there anything else I can assist you with today?" sound corporate in a rural setting. The tone should be warm, genuine, and informal: "Let me know if you need anything — I'm just in the garden."
- **Edge case — guest who does not want interaction**: some guests book a rural retreat specifically for solitude. Respect this. Deliver the welcome amenity and a short note: "Everything you need is in the room — the kitchen serves dinner at 19:00, no reservation needed. We're here if you need us, and happy to give you space if you prefer." This is also a form of excellent service.
- **Albastru / Șapte Focuri context**: the greatest competitive advantage is authenticity. The open-fire kitchen, the owner greeting, the local products, the handwritten notes — these cannot be replicated by chain hotels. The framework should amplify what already exists naturally, not manufacture artificial luxury. Cornell's research consistently shows that authenticity scores higher than polish in the boutique segment. The goal is to be intentional about what already makes the property special, not to become something it is not.
- **Revenue impact**: Cornell data shows a 1-point improvement in guest satisfaction (on a 5-point scale) correlates with a 3–6% increase in willingness to pay premium rates. A guest experience framework is not a cost center — it is the primary revenue driver for a property that competes on experience rather than price.
