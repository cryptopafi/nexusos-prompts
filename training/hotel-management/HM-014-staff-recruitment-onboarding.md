# Staff Recruitment and Onboarding for Hospitality

## Obiectiv
Attract, select, and integrate hospitality staff who align with the property's service culture — targeting a first-year retention rate of 75%+, a time-to-productive of 14 days for operational roles, and a 30/60/90-day probation pass rate of 85%+ by investing in a rigorous, culture-first hiring and onboarding process.

## Pași

### Pas 1: Define the Role Before Advertising
- Write a **Job Description** that is specific, honest, and attractive:
  - **Role title and department**: use standard hospitality titles (Front Desk Agent, Housekeeping Attendant, Chef de Partie, Restaurant Server). Avoid creative titles that confuse candidates.
  - **Reporting line**: who they report to directly.
  - **5–8 core responsibilities**: specific and measurable, not vague. "Greet guests within 30 seconds and complete check-in within 8 minutes" instead of "welcome guests."
  - **Required qualifications**: minimum education (high school for operational roles; degree for management), certifications (food safety/HACCP for kitchen, first aid for front desk), language skills (Romanian fluent; English conversational minimum for guest-facing roles; other languages as bonus).
  - **Experience**: be realistic. For entry-level roles, require 0–1 years. For supervisory roles, 2–3 years. For management, 5+ years. Inflated experience requirements exclude good candidates and signal that you do not invest in training.
  - **Schedule transparency**: state the actual schedule requirements honestly. "This role requires weekend and holiday shifts. The schedule rotates weekly and is published 2 weeks in advance." Candidates who self-select out based on schedule are candidates who would have quit in month 2.
  - **Compensation transparency**: include the salary range or hourly rate. In Romania, publishing salary ranges is increasingly expected and improves application quality. Include: base salary, estimated tips/service charge, meal during shift, any benefits (transport, housing assistance for rural properties, employee discount).
  - **3 culture-fit statements**: describe the human qualities that matter. "You genuinely enjoy making people feel welcome." "You stay calm and kind under pressure." "You notice the small details that others miss — a crooked picture frame, an empty water glass." These statements attract the right people and filter the wrong ones.
- Get approval: every JD must be reviewed by the department head and GM before posting. This prevents role creep and ensures alignment with the staffing model (HM-012).

### Pas 2: Sourcing Strategy — Finding the Right People
- Source through multiple channels simultaneously. Different roles require different sourcing strategies:
- **Front Office, F&B Service, Guest Relations**:
  - eJobs.ro, BestJobs.ro (Romania's primary job boards — post with clear title, salary range, and location).
  - LinkedIn (for experienced hires and management roles).
  - Local hospitality school partnerships: Liceul Tehnologic / Colegiu de Turism in nearby cities. Establish an internship-to-hire pipeline (3-month internship, convert top performers to employment). This is the most reliable long-term source for rural properties.
  - Instagram: post hiring stories and reels showing the work environment. "Join our team at Sapte Focuri — here's what it's like to work in the most unique kitchen in Romania." Visual hiring posts attract candidates who identify with the brand.
  - Employee referrals: the single best source for culture-fit candidates. Existing staff who recommend a friend are putting their own reputation on the line — this natural filter is powerful.
- **Housekeeping, Kitchen Porter, Maintenance**:
  - Local community notice boards, church bulletins, and word-of-mouth.
  - Local employment agencies (AJOFM — Agentia Judeteana pentru Ocuparea Fortei de Munca).
  - Direct referrals from current staff.
  - Social media: Facebook groups for local employment.
- **Chefs and Kitchen Staff**:
  - Culinary schools (USAMV Bucharest gastronomy program, private culinary academies).
  - LinkedIn and industry networking events (HoReCa trade shows).
  - Chef communities and Facebook groups (Romanian chef groups are active).
  - Direct outreach to chefs at similar-level restaurants (poaching ethically — contact professionals who are visibly seeking new opportunities).
- **Department Heads / Management**:
  - LinkedIn headhunting: search for candidates at comparable properties.
  - Hotel management association networks (FIHR — Federatia Industriei Hoteliere din Romania).
  - Executive search firms for senior roles (Cost: 2–3 months' salary — justified for critical hires like Executive Chef or Hotel Manager).
- **Employee Referral Program**: reward referrals that convert to successful hires. Structure: 500–1,000 RON bonus paid in two installments — 50% at hire, 50% after the referred employee completes 90 days. This ensures the referrer only gets full payment for referrals who actually stay. Announce referral bonuses at team meetings and remind staff quarterly.

### Pas 3: Screening and Interview Process
- **Application screening** (5 minutes per application):
  - Filter on hard requirements only: can they work the required schedule? Do they have the minimum language skills? Are they legally able to work in Romania?
  - Do NOT over-filter at this stage. In hospitality, attitude and energy are more important than a polished CV. A candidate with no hotel experience but strong customer service skills (retail, restaurant) can be trained.
- **Phone screen** (15 minutes):
  - Confirm: availability for required schedule, compensation expectations aligned with your range, transport/housing situation (critical for rural properties — can they reliably get to work?).
  - One behavioral question: "Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult customer or a stressful situation at work. What happened and what did you do?"
  - Energy check: are they enthusiastic? Do they ask questions about the property? A candidate who is flat on a phone screen will not light up in front of guests.
  - If basics align, invite to in-person interview within 5 business days (do not delay — good candidates are snatched quickly in hospitality).
- **In-person interview** (45–60 minutes):
  - **Property tour first** (10 minutes): walk the candidate through the property before the formal interview. Watch how they interact with the environment: do they notice details? Do they smile at staff they pass? Do they ask questions about the space? The property tour reveals more about hospitality instinct than any interview question.
  - **Behavioral interview questions** (STAR format: Situation, Task, Action, Result):
    - "Tell me about your best day at work in hospitality — what made it great?"
    - "Describe a time you went above and beyond for a guest or customer."
    - "Tell me about a time you made a mistake at work. How did you handle it?"
    - "Have you ever had a disagreement with a colleague? How was it resolved?"
    - "What does 'attention to detail' mean to you? Give me an example."
  - **Scenario question** (reveals problem-solving and service instinct): "A guest arrives at 14:00 to check in. Their room is not ready because the previous guest checked out late. The arriving guest is tired from a long drive and visibly frustrated. What do you do?" Look for: empathy first, then action (luggage storage, complimentary drink, specific time commitment), proactive communication. Red flags: blame ("that shouldn't happen"), indifference ("just tell them to wait"), rigidity ("our policy says check-in is at 15:00").
  - **Present the role's realities honestly**: shift patterns, busy periods, physical demands (standing for 8 hours, lifting linen bags, working in heat near the fire). A candidate who self-selects out after hearing the truth saves you a hire-and-quit cycle.
  - **Assess culture fit**: would you want this person representing your property to a guest? Would your existing team enjoy working alongside them? Trust your instinct on culture fit — a technically skilled person who does not fit the culture will undermine the team.
- **Trial shift** (mandatory for all guest-facing and kitchen roles): 4-hour paid trial shift in the actual role environment.
  - Front desk: process a mock check-in with feedback from the supervisor.
  - F&B service: work a real service alongside an experienced server.
  - Kitchen: prepare 2–3 dishes from the current menu with the head chef observing.
  - Housekeeping: clean 2 rooms to standard with supervisor inspection.
  - Evaluate: speed, attention to detail, interaction with existing team, response to real-time feedback, attitude when corrected.
  - Pay the trial shift at the standard hourly rate. It is not free labor — it is a paid assessment. Unpaid trials are both legally questionable and ethically wrong.

### Pas 4: Making the Offer and Legal Compliance
- **Verbal offer** within 48 hours of the final interview/trial shift decision. Call the candidate personally — do not email a first offer. "We were impressed with your trial shift and we'd like to offer you the position. Here's what we're proposing..." Confirm: role, start date, salary/hourly rate, schedule, probation period, reporting structure.
- **Written offer letter** within 24 hours of verbal acceptance. Content: role title, department, start date, gross salary and net estimate, probation period (standard 90 days in Romania per the Labor Code), working hours and shift structure, reporting line, and any specific conditions.
- **Legal compliance (Romania-specific)**:
  - **CIM (Contract Individual de Munca)**: the employment contract must be signed before the first working day. Not after — before. An employee working without a registered contract is a significant legal violation (fines: 10,000–20,000 RON per unregistered employee).
  - **REVISAL registration**: the employer must register the employment contract in REVISAL (the electronic employee register maintained by ITM — Inspectia Muncii) at least one day before the employee starts work.
  - **Required documents from the employee**: ID card/passport, SSM (health and safety) medical fitness certificate from an occupational medicine provider, tax identification form (declaratie fiscala), bank account details for salary transfer.
  - **Probation period**: 90 calendar days for execution roles, 120 days for management roles. During probation, either party can terminate with notice per the contract (typically 20 days for employees, 30 days for employer).
- **Rejection notifications**: respond to ALL candidates who interviewed within 10 business days, even if the answer is no. A professional rejection preserves the property's reputation: "Thank you for your interest in [property name]. We appreciated meeting you, but we've decided to move forward with another candidate whose experience more closely matched our current need. We'll keep your details on file for future opportunities."

### Pas 5: Onboarding Program — The Critical First 14 Days
- **Pre-arrival preparation** (before day 1):
  - Workspace/locker assigned. Uniform ordered and sized. Name badge prepared.
  - PMS/POS access created with minimum required permissions.
  - First-week schedule prepared and sent to the new hire.
  - Buddy assigned: an experienced team member in the same department who will be the new hire's go-to person for the first month. Brief the buddy on their role.
  - Welcome packet assembled: employee handbook, property map, emergency procedures, wifi password, staff meal schedule, parking instructions.
- **Day 1 — Welcome and Orientation** (full day, no operational duties):
  - 09:00: welcome by the GM or direct manager. 30-minute personal introduction — property history, values, what makes this place special, what is expected of every team member. "Every person who works here is an ambassador for this property. Our guests should feel that every interaction — whether it's at the front desk, in the restaurant, or passing you in the corridor — is warm, genuine, and attentive."
  - 10:00: full property tour with the buddy. Every department, every space guests use, staff areas, emergency exits, fire assembly point, first aid kit locations, emergency telephone numbers.
  - 11:00: issue uniform, locker key, name badge. Set up system access (PMS login, email, scheduling app).
  - 12:00: staff lunch with the team — informal introduction.
  - 13:00: employee handbook review. Key policies: dress code, grooming, punctuality, shift swap procedure, guest interaction standards, social media policy (can they post about the property?), complaint handling basics, health and safety (fire evacuation, first aid, chemical handling for housekeeping).
  - 14:00: department-specific orientation with the department head. Overview of the department's daily routine, key procedures, and quality standards. Provide the relevant SOPs (HM-001 for FO, HM-002 for housekeeping, HM-003 for F&B).
  - 15:00: health and safety training (SSM/PSI — mandatory in Romania, documented with signed acknowledgment).
  - 16:00: close day 1 with a 15-minute check-in. "How was your first day? Any questions? Anything that was unclear?"
- **Days 2–5 — Shadowing** (observe, assist, learn):
  - The new hire shadows their buddy through real shifts. They observe, ask questions, and assist with supervised tasks. No solo guest interaction yet.
  - By end of day 3: the new hire should be able to explain the department's daily workflow from start to finish.
  - By end of day 5: the new hire should be able to perform basic tasks with supervision (check-in under guidance, clean a room with inspection, serve a table with senior server support).
- **Days 6–10 — Supervised Practice**:
  - The new hire performs tasks with decreasing supervision. Buddy observes and provides immediate feedback.
  - Front desk: process 5+ check-ins with buddy standing alongside.
  - Housekeeping: clean rooms solo with supervisor inspection of every room.
  - F&B: manage a section (2–3 tables) during service with backup available.
  - End of day 10: supervisor assessment. "Can this person perform the core duties of the role safely and to minimum standard without constant supervision?"
- **Days 11–14 — Independent Practice with Safety Net**:
  - The new hire works independently but knows exactly who to call if they encounter something they cannot handle.
  - Buddy remains available but does not shadow.
  - Day 14: formal end-of-onboarding review with the supervisor. Set 30-day development goals: 3 specific, measurable targets (e.g., "Achieve 8.0+ inspection score on all rooms cleaned," "Attempt upsell on every check-in," "Memorize all menu items and allergens").

### Pas 6: Probation Period Management (30/60/90 days)
- **30-day check-in** (formal 1-on-1, 30 minutes):
  - Review the 30-day goals set on day 14. Progress? Achieved? Need adjustment?
  - Performance data: check-in speed (FO), inspection score average (housekeeping), cover output (F&B), punctuality record, guest feedback mentions.
  - Morale and integration: "How are you finding the work? The team? Is there anything you need that would help you perform better?"
  - If performance is on track: acknowledge progress positively. Set 60-day goals.
  - If performance is below expectations: specific, behavioral feedback. "Your room inspection scores are averaging 6.8 — the standard is 8.0. Here's what's being missed most frequently: [specific items]. Let's schedule extra training time this week."
- **60-day check-in** (formal 1-on-1, 30 minutes):
  - Performance assessment against role KPIs. By day 60, the employee should be performing at 80%+ of a fully competent team member's level.
  - Behavioral assessment: how do they interact with guests? With colleagues? Do they take initiative or wait to be told?
  - If performance is strong: confirm trajectory toward passing probation. Discuss development path.
  - If performance is marginal: deliver clear, documented feedback. "Here are the specific areas that need to reach standard by day 90 for us to confirm your employment." Provide additional training/mentoring.
- **90-day review** (formal assessment, go/no-go decision):
  - Full performance review against all role criteria.
  - **Pass** (meets or exceeds expectations): confirm permanent employment. Update REVISAL. Discuss career development aspirations. "Congratulations — we're happy to confirm your position. You've become a valuable part of the team."
  - **Conditional pass** (mostly meets but 1–2 areas need improvement): extend probation by 30 days with a written improvement plan specifying exactly what must change and by when. Legal note: in Romania, probation extension beyond the statutory period requires employee agreement.
  - **Fail** (below standard despite support): end employment per the probation terms. Be direct, respectful, and specific. "Despite the training and support we've provided, your performance has not reached the standard we need for this role. We've decided not to continue past the probation period." Provide the required notice. Never retain staff who are clearly wrong for the role out of short-term convenience — the long-term cost (guest experience damage, team morale, management time) far exceeds the short-term pain of replacement.

### Pas 7: Retention — Why Good People Stay
- Hiring is expensive (estimated cost of replacing one hospitality employee: 3–6 months' salary when you include recruitment, training, productivity loss, and service quality dip). Retention is the higher-ROI investment.
- **Retention drivers in hospitality** (in order of impact per research):
  1. **Fair scheduling**: publish schedules 2 weeks in advance. Allow shift swaps with manager approval. Respect time-off requests whenever possible. Unpredictable scheduling is the #1 resignation driver in hospitality.
  2. **Recognition**: specific, public, timely. "Maria cleaned 14 rooms today and every single one scored 9.0 or above — let's give her a round of applause at team meeting." Quarterly "Employee of the Month" with a visible reward (certificate, bonus, preferred parking, dinner for two).
  3. **Growth opportunity**: even in a small property, people want to see a future. Create development paths: housekeeping attendant → senior attendant → housekeeping supervisor. Server → senior server → F&B supervisor. Invest in training (wine certification, fire safety instructor, front desk management course).
  4. **Fair compensation**: pay at or above market rate. In a rural area with limited competition for labor, you may be the best employer — leverage this. Review compensation annually.
  5. **Team culture**: regular team activities (monthly staff lunch, annual team outing, birthday recognition). A team that enjoys working together retains better.
  6. **Physical working conditions**: staff areas matter. A clean, comfortable break room with proper seating, a microwave, a fridge, and a coffee machine shows respect. Staff who eat standing in a dirty corridor do not feel valued.
- **Exit interviews**: when an employee resigns, conduct a 20-minute exit interview. Standard questions: "What was the main reason for your decision?" "What could we have done differently?" "Would you recommend working here to a friend?" Document insights and look for patterns across exits. If 3 of the last 5 departures cite the same issue, that issue must be addressed.

## Note
- **"Hire for attitude, train for skill"** — the most important principle in hospitality recruitment. Technical skills (PMS operation, bed-making technique, wine service) are teachable within 14 days. Warmth, resilience, attention to detail, and a genuine desire to serve are personality traits that cannot be taught. If a candidate has the right attitude but limited experience, hire them and invest in training. If a candidate has 10 years of experience but a flat personality, pass.
- **Common mistake — rushing the hire**: a vacant position creates pressure to "just get someone in." Resist. A bad hire costs 3–6 months of salary in replacement costs and damages guest experience and team morale during their tenure. It is better to run short-staffed for 2 weeks than to hire someone who will fail in month 2.
- **Edge case — candidate is great but cannot commute reliably**: for rural properties, transport is often the decisive factor. If the candidate is exceptional but lives 40+ minutes away without reliable transport, consider: providing a staff shuttle (shared with other staff), offering accommodation for shift days (a staff room costs 10–15 RON/night in forgone revenue but saves a hire), or adjusting the schedule to reduce commute frequency (longer shifts, fewer days).
- **Albastru / Sapte Focuri context**: for a boutique property in a rural/semi-rural location, sourcing is the hardest part. Invest in the local community — partner with nearby vocational schools (Liceul Tehnologic) that offer hospitality or gastronomy programs. A structured internship program (3-month internship with a genuine learning plan, fair compensation, and a conversion path to employment) is the most reliable long-term pipeline. Returning seasonal staff are gold — maintain relationships year-round (holiday greeting, first-call for next season). For the kitchen specifically, the Sapte Focuri concept attracts chefs who are passionate about traditional cooking — use the uniqueness of the kitchen as a recruitment tool: "There is no other kitchen like this in Romania. If you want to learn traditional open-fire cooking with clay pots, this is the only place to do it." For very small teams (5–8 people), every hire matters disproportionately — one wrong person disrupts the entire team dynamic. Be selective even when the pressure to fill is high.
